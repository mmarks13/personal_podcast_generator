## A generated planet
A: Happily, because this one delighted me, and I want to explain why it's more than a graphics demo. It's called ABot-Earth — version zero-point-five — up on Hugging Face's daily papers. The pitch: hand it satellite imagery of anywhere on Earth, and it produces a full three-dimensional scene you could fly a camera through — buildings, streets, textures — at a rate of under ten minutes per square kilometer.
B: Under ten minutes a square kilometer. Okay, my builder brain immediately wants to know one thing — generates, or reconstructs? Because those are very different verbs.
A: [pleased] That is exactly the right question, and it's the whole story. But hold it for a second, because I want to give you the lineage first — this is one of those cases where knowing the path makes the trick obvious.
B: Go.
A: So how do you normally turn photographs into three-D? The old craft is photogrammetry. You take many overlapping photos of a thing from different angles and you triangulate — this pixel and that pixel are the same corner of the same building, so that corner must sit here in space. That's how Google Earth's three-D was built. It's a survey. It's measurement.
B: And it needs a lot of angles. You can't triangulate a wall you never photographed.
A: Right. Then around 2020 came neural radiance fields — NeRF — which learned a little function that, given any camera position, tells you what you'd see from there. Gorgeous results, but slow to train, slow to render. And then in 2023 the field mostly leapt to a thing called Gaussian splatting.
B: Which is — help me say it in builder terms.
A: Instead of one big network, you represent the whole scene as millions of tiny fuzzy colored blobs floating in three-D space. Little translucent smudges, each with a position, a color, a size. To draw a frame, you splat them onto the screen. It's fast, and you can tune the blobs directly — nudge each one's position and color until the whole cloud matches your photos from every angle.
B: So — millions of little translucent paint blobs, and you optimize them like puzzle pieces until they look right. [chuckles] How many are we talking?
A: In their full production run — about three-point-two trillion of them.
B: Three-point-two trillion paint blobs. Alright, that's a number.
A: But — everything I've described so far is still reconstruction. It's survey. You need the photos. Here's the leap ABot-Earth makes, and it's your verb question coming home. They train a generative model directly in that blob representation, on a big corpus of real three-D city reconstructions. So the model learns a prior — a deep sense of what cities are shaped like. And then at inference, it generates the scene conditioned only on satellite imagery. Mostly top-down imagery.
B: [skeptical] Wait. Top-down. So it's looking essentially straight down at a city — and inventing the sides of the buildings?
A: Inventing is the honest word. It has never seen those facades. It produces the most plausible facades given everything it learned about how buildings look.
B: So this is not a map. This is a very well-educated hallucination of a place.
A: [chuckles] That's the caution, and I'd put it exactly that strongly. What comes back is plausible, not surveyed.
B: But how do you even train that? Your real three-D reconstructions were built from rich ground-level and aerial photos. At inference you only get the flat top-down satellite view. That's a mismatch.
A: And that's the neat part of the engineering. During training they take their real three-D scenes and render them from a simulated satellite's-eye view — straight down — then pair that fake overhead image with the true three-D underneath it. So the model spends training learning exactly the inverse map you'd want: from a top-down look, to a full standing world. They bridge the gap on purpose instead of hoping it generalizes.
B: Ah — so they manufacture the hard case and drill on it. That's clean.
A: And they build it with levels of detail baked in — coarse at city scale, sharpening as you zoom to a street — so it streams like a real map application instead of loading a whole city at once. If you're a planner checking one specific building's real height, still the wrong tool. But if you need a convincing three-dimensional world, fast, everywhere — it's remarkable.
B: Give me the number behind "remarkable," though, because that word is doing a lot of lifting.
A: Fair. They use a standard image-realism score — lower is better — and they report about sixteen, against roughly seventy for the next-best system in their comparison. That's a wide gap on that metric. Authors' own numbers, their own benchmark, so — external replication pending, as always with these.
B: And the coverage?
A: Three hundred-plus cities across more than a hundred and ninety countries. And the production math is the part that made me sit up. The built-up surface of the entire planet is roughly eight hundred thousand square kilometers — and they describe running that whole thing on a thousand-GPU cluster in under ten days.
B: The entire built world. In ten days. [pause] Okay, that reframes the pitch. It isn't "a beautiful model of one city." It's "a plausible three-D everywhere, refreshable on a schedule Google Earth can't match." And they make that comparison directly — that Earth's imagery can be months or years stale.
A: And that's the so-what. Disaster response is the application they lead with, and it's the one that lands for me. A flood, an earthquake — you get fresh satellite imagery within hours, and you could stand up a navigable three-D model of the hit zone the same day. Drones route through it, responders plan against it. Staleness is the enemy in that scenario, and generation beats survey on staleness every time.
B: As long as everyone using it remembers the walls are guesses.
A: [warmly] Which is the whole discipline of this show, really — knowing exactly how far to trust the thing that looks authoritative.
## Grading code without running it
B: Alright, mine. Squarely in the coding-agent world, and it's a clever answer to a boring, expensive problem. The paper is called Dockerless.
A: Dockerless. As in — without Docker, the container tool.
B: Exactly. So, set up the problem. When you train a coding agent — one of these things that takes a bug report and writes a patch — you need a way, during training, to tell it whether the patch it just wrote was any good. That signal is what it learns from. And the gold-standard signal is dead simple: apply the patch, run the project's test suite, see if the tests pass.
A: Which sounds cheap. Running tests is the one thing computers are supposed to be good at.
B: Running them is cheap. Setting up to run them is brutal. For every single repository you need an isolated environment — the right language version, every dependency pinned, the test harness wired up correctly. That's what Docker is for. And the paper's whole point is that building those environments is where the pain lives. It fails constantly. And for private, or legacy, or messy enterprise codebases, it's often just not possible to reproduce the environment at all.
A: So there's this huge class of real code you simply can't train on — because you can't stand up a clean room to test it in.
B: That's the gap. And here's their move: don't run anything. Verify the patch by reading. They point a second agent at the repository with only read-only tools — grep, find, that kind of thing — and it writes itself a handful of diagnostic questions. Where should this fix actually apply? What would a correct patch touch? Which tests, if they existed, would confirm it? Sub-agents go spelunking through the real code to answer those, and then a judge model reads all that evidence, plus the patch, and outputs a score — how likely is this correct.
A: [skeptical] Alan. We have spent this entire week on studies saying models are worse at checking than at doing. The whole "generation is cheap, verification is the bottleneck" drum. And this is — verification by reading comprehension. Why should I believe the reader is any good?
B: [laughs] I knew you'd swing at that, and it's the right swing. The answer's in the numbers. On a benchmark of judging patches, their reader beats the best open-source verifier by about fourteen points on a standard scoring metric. But the number that matters more — when they use this reader to actually train a coding agent, end to end, with no environments anywhere in the loop, the resulting model hits sixty-two percent on SWE-bench Verified. That's the standard bug-fixing benchmark.
A: Sixty-two versus what? The number's meaningless on its own.
B: Versus about sixty-two-and-a-half for the exact same pipeline trained the old way, with real test execution. That's the headline. It essentially matches environment-based training — without ever building an environment.
A: [surprised] Huh. So the reader isn't perfect, but it's a good enough teacher that the student comes out in the same place.
B: That's the finding. And the reading step is cheap — it adds something like seven percent to the training time, against the enormous overhead of building all those containers.
A: So where does it break? "Reading instead of running" has to have a tell.
B: It does, and to their credit they name it. Compilation-heavy languages — Rust, C — it runs seven to thirteen points worse there. Because when you actually run a compiler, it tells you the truth in a way reading the source just can't. No amount of grep replaces a type error the compiler would have caught for free.
A: That's the honest seam — and it's the historian's note, too. This isn't "checking turned out to be easy after all." It's narrower. For a class of high-level languages where correctness is mostly logic and structure, a careful reader is good enough to teach with. And notice — they still needed a few thousand examples labeled by real test execution to build that reader in the first place. Execution didn't vanish. It moved upstream.
B: Right. It's bootstrapped on the old expensive signal, then amortized across everything after. Which is a very different claim from "we don't need tests anymore."
A: But a genuinely useful one. If you're a shop sitting on a mountain of code you could never containerize, this is the difference between training on it and not.
B: Your untestable legacy repo just turned into training data. That's the unlock.

## The week in review
A: So pull all the way back. If I had to name what this week actually established, it's that the industry has quietly stopped arguing about whether the models are smart enough — and started arguing about everything that surrounds them.
B: Which is a real shift. Rewind six months and every week was a capability week — new model, higher benchmark. This week, almost none of it was about raw intelligence.
A: Three things hardened, I think. First: the bottleneck is deployment and checking, not capability. We read it from every direction. Papers showing agents can't hold a plan across a long task, can't tell when to stop. Productivity studies — including today's — where the time saved doesn't convert to anything. And the money voting the same way: Amazon, Microsoft, the labs, all standing up billion-dollar organizations whose entire job is getting AI to actually work inside a company. When Zuckerberg told his own staff on Thursday that agents hadn't accelerated the way he'd hoped, that wasn't an outlier — it was the week's consensus, spoken by the person with the most reason to say the opposite.
