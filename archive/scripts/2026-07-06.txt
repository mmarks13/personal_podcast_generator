## Amazon retires the Turk
A: So — TechCrunch reported over the weekend that Amazon is closing Mechanical Turk to new customers. As of July thirtieth, no new sign-ups. Existing customers can keep going, but Amazon says it won't add any new features. Not a hard shutdown — more of a do-not-resuscitate order.
B: And for anyone who never touched it — what was Mechanical Turk?
A: It launched in 2005. It's a marketplace where you post tiny tasks that computers were bad at, and thousands of people around the world do them for pennies each. Label this image. Is this sentence angry or happy. Transcribe this receipt. And Amazon marketed it — this is their own phrase — as "artificial artificial intelligence."
B: Named after the eighteenth-century thing. The chess automaton.
A: And that's the whole joke folded in on itself. In the seventeen-seventies a Hungarian inventor built a machine — a figure at a chessboard, dressed as a Turk — that supposedly played chess on its own and beat nobles all over Europe. It was a hoax. There was a human chess master hidden inside, working the arm through the cabinet.
B: So the original Mechanical Turk was a machine that was secretly a person.
A: And Amazon named its human-labor marketplace after it, on purpose, with a wink. Because for two decades the service was the hidden person inside a lot of things sold as artificial intelligence. TechCrunch makes the point directly — it was the quiet enabler for the fake-it-till-you-make-it era, where a startup demos "the AI" and it's really Turk workers answering in the background.
B: [wry] And there's something a little on the nose about the two of us narrating the retirement of the humans who used to pretend to be us.
A: [chuckles] I noticed that too. The Turk hid a person inside a machine. We're the other order.
B: [skeptical] Okay, so why does it die now? If it's cheap human labor for the stuff machines can't do — the machines got good, demand dried up. That's the obvious story.
A: That's half of it, and it's the poetic half. But there's a darker twist, and it's the part I'd actually sit with. TechCrunch cites a 2023 analysis that found somewhere between a third and a half of Mechanical Turk workers were themselves using large language models to do the tasks.
B: [laughs] Wait. So you're paying humans, specifically because you want human answers and not machine answers — and the humans are quietly asking a machine.
A: The snake eats its tail. The entire value of the platform was a human in the loop. And the human put a language model in the loop instead — which poisons exactly the thing you were buying. If you were collecting data to train your model, and the labels were secretly written by another model, you're just training on machine output laundered through a person.
B: And that's a real, current problem, not a cute irony. An enormous amount of this field's training and evaluation data came from platforms like this. If you're a team that used crowd labels — and almost everyone did, at some point — that's a reminder that a chunk of your "human" ground truth may not have been.
A: And it names the shift underneath. The reason you don't spin up a Mechanical Turk in 2026 is that for a lot of these tasks, you now just call the model directly. Synthetic data, model-graded labels. We spent a chunk of last week on a coding-agent verifier that grades patches by reading instead of running — same instinct. The cheap human in the loop gets replaced by the model it was already pretending to be.
B: Which is a genuine loss, in one specific way. The messy, disagreeing, distribution-of-real-people signal — that's the thing synthetic data doesn't have. When your labels come from a model, they carry the model's blind spots, cleanly, at scale, and they all agree with each other.
A: That's the caution I'd land on. Mechanical Turk was ethically fraught — pennies for piecework, no protections — nobody should romanticize it. But it was a narrow window onto what actual humans think, and we're boarding that window up right as we start trusting models to tell us what humans would have said.
## Does the AI tutor actually work
B: Second one. There's a paper that hit Hacker News hard this weekend, out of Dartmouth, and it's one of the few AI-in-education studies with real exam numbers attached — not a survey, not vibes. A researcher named Jonah Bard built a learning platform called Phosphor and ran it inside an actual course.
A: Real course, real students?
B: Introductory Statistics at Dartmouth — the one non-math-majors take. Three sections, about a hundred and fifty students, this past spring. And here's the important part: the platform is not a chatbot. It's the textbook, rebuilt so that as you read, you hit quizzes embedded right in the material — and a model, Claude Sonnet, grades your written answers against a rubric the instructor wrote.
A: So the AI isn't explaining anything. The AI is grading.
B: The AI is grading. Short-answer questions, at scale, instantly, with feedback. That's the thing you could never do before without an army of teaching assistants. And here's the headline, the reason it's making the rounds: students who fully engaged with the material scored — depending on how you adjust — between about seven-tenths and one-and-a-third of a standard deviation higher on the final exam.
A: [skeptical] Okay, Alan, I need to slow you down, because that's an enormous number and my alarm is going off. A standard deviation and a third is the gap between a middling student and a top one. What's the comparison group? Because if this is just "students who chose to do all the optional work did better" — that's not the AI. That's the good students being good students.
B: [laughs] You went straight for the throat, and it's exactly the right question — and to their credit, so do the authors. This is an observational study. No random assignment. They name self-selection as the central threat, in those words.
A: So how do they defend the number?
B: They control for prior performance — how you did on the earlier midterms. Unadjusted, the gap is that one-point-three standard deviations. Once you control for midterm scores, it drops to about zero-point-seven-one. And they're honest that the real effect is somewhere in that range — they actually call zero-point-seven-one a conservative floor, not the answer.
A: A floor of zero-point-seven is still large for education, I'll grant that. But I want the mechanism, because "AI grades your homework and you learn more" isn't a mechanism. It's a correlation wearing a hopeful label.
B: And this is the part I loved, because the study accidentally ran its own controlled experiment. Partway through, students complained the written-answer grader was too harsh. So for one module, they switched to multiple-choice only. Then later switched back.
A: Oh — that's a natural experiment handed to them on a plate.
B: Right. And the result is stark. In the modules with written, constructed-response questions — where you have to produce the answer out of your own head — more practice tracked higher exam scores. In the multiple-choice-only module, that relationship completely vanished. Same platform, same kind of engaged students, zero dosage effect the moment the questions became "pick A, B, or C."
A: [interested] Huh. Now that's a mechanism — and notice it's not about the AI at all. That's the testing effect. The doer effect, in the education literature. The act of retrieving an answer, of generating it yourself, is what lays down the memory. Recognizing the right option in a list does almost nothing by comparison. And that's not new — that result goes back decades.
B: Which reframes the whole story. The reason this worked isn't that a language model is a magic tutor. It's that a language model is finally cheap enough to grade open-ended answers at scale — so you can make a few thousand students write instead of click. The AI is the enabler for old, boring, extremely well-established teaching.
A: The opposite of the usual pitch. And there's one more detail that's the real tell. Did this thing have a chatbot in it?
B: It did. A retrieval chat assistant — ask-the-textbook-a-question, right there in the sidebar.
A: And?
B: Almost nobody used it. Seventy-two queries, total, across the entire course. Students basically said: if I want to chat, I'll just use ChatGPT — the reference material was enough.
A: [laughs] Which is the quietly damning finding buried inside a positive paper. The feature everyone rushes to build — the conversational tutor — got ignored. The thing that actually moved exam scores was the unglamorous grader making you do practice problems.
B: So the honest takeaway for anyone building education tools: the win isn't the tutor that talks. It's the assessment that makes the student generate. Now — one selective school, one pilot, no randomization. The authors would be first to tell you not to over-read it. But the direction is a genuinely useful corrective.
A: And it rhymes with the thing we say constantly. The value showed up in the checking, not the generating.
## Three tricks, one dial
A: Last real one, and it's for the people who train these models — but stay with me, because the idea underneath is elegant and you don't need any of the math. There's a paper going around whose title is basically the whole abstract: "GRPO, Dr. GRPO, and DAPO are three operations on one number."
B: Those are three reinforcement-learning methods. Three recipes for teaching a model to reason.
A: Right — the family of techniques behind a lot of this year's reasoning models. And in the field they're treated as three rival tricks, three separate papers, and teams genuinely agonize over which to use. This paper proves they're the same trick, with one knob set three different ways.
B: Okay. Teach me. What's the one number?
A: The setup is simpler than the acronyms make it sound. To train one of these models, you take a problem, and you have the model answer it many times — say, sixteen attempts. An automatic checker marks each attempt right or wrong. So now you've got a group of sixteen answers, some right, some wrong. And the question is: how much should the model learn from this one problem?
B: And the naive intuition is — reinforce the ones it got right, push away from the ones it got wrong.
A: But look at the group as a whole. Take the two extremes. If all sixteen come back right — the model already knows this cold. Nothing to teach. If all sixteen come back wrong — it has no toehold, no correct example to pull toward. Also nothing to teach.
B: So the sweet spot's in the middle. Eight right, eight wrong.
A: Maximum disagreement — the problem the model is right on the knife's edge of, where a nudge actually moves it. And here's the identity at the heart of the paper. For right-or-wrong grading, the standard deviation of those marks — the statistician's measure of how much the group disagrees — comes out to a clean little formula. Biggest when the group splits evenly. Exactly zero when they all agree.
B: So disagreement is the whole signal. High when it's a coin flip, zero when it's unanimous.
A: And the punchline is that this disagreement number isn't some side-calculation bolted onto the training. For these binary rewards, it is the size of the learning update. A split group teaches the most. A unanimous group teaches nothing — the authors' own phrase is that it "falls silent."
B: [understanding] And so the three methods —
A: — are just three ways of handling that one number. The original one, GRPO, divides by it. The second, Dr. GRPO — GRPO "done right" — says stop dividing by it. And the third, DAPO, says throw away the groups where it's zero, because a silent group is wasted compute anyway. Three papers. One dial.
B: [skeptical] Alright, let me push, though — because "it's all secretly the same" papers can be a little too pleased with themselves. Does this change anything I'd actually do? Or is it a lovely unification that leaves my job exactly where it was?
A: Fair, and that's the part I'd defend — it's not just aesthetics. Once you know the update size is literally that formula, you can compute things you used to eyeball. Like: how many times do I need to sample each problem before the signal is trustworthy? And what fraction of my groups are going to come back unanimous and teach nothing — so I know how much compute I'm about to burn for free? Both of those fall straight out of the identity, as closed forms. And they check it on a big real-world math dataset and a controlled training run.
B: So it turns three folk remedies into one thing you can actually reason about. That I'll take. The knob was always there — they just proved it's one knob, and told you what it's wired to.
A: And historically, that's the satisfying kind of result. Not a new capability — a clarification. Somebody noticed three things everyone treated as separate were one object, seen from three angles. The field does that far too rarely.
