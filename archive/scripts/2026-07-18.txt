## Trust nurses, not AI
A: So. A Bay Area outlet, Local News Matters, published a piece this week — it went to the front page of Hacker News, five hundred points, three hundred and forty comments, so it clearly hit a nerve — about advice nurses at Kaiser Permanente, and the monitoring they say they're working under.

## The plumbing behind a two-million-token agent
B: Second real story, and it's mine, and I promise the payoff is bigger than the topic sounds. There's a paper called LongStraw. It's about training — reinforcement-learning training — on really long contexts. Millions of tokens.
A: Set up the problem first. Why is this a problem at all?
B: Here's the gap the paper opens with, and it's genuinely odd once you see it. On the serving side — inference, actually running the model to answer you — we're pushing toward million-token contexts. That's normal now. But the training that comes after pre-training — the reinforcement-learning step where you tune the model's behavior — mostly still happens at two hundred fifty-six thousand tokens or below.
A: So we train short and deploy long.
B: We train short and deploy long. And that's fine until you care about agents. Because an agent on a long task accumulates — every tool output, every observation, every thing it did four hundred steps ago, all piling up in the context. The behavior you most want to train — "make good decisions deep into a long, messy run" — is exactly the behavior your training is too short to ever show the model.
A: So why not just train long? What stops them?
B: Memory. And here's the piece worth actually understanding, because it's the whole story. When you run a model forward — inference — you can throw away your work as you go. Token by token, you don't need to remember how you computed the last one. But training is different. To train, you do a backward pass — you compute the answer, then you walk all the way back through every step to work out which knob to nudge. And to walk back, you have to have kept the entire forward computation in memory. Every intermediate value, for every token.
A: So the memory cost of training scales with length in a way inference just doesn't.
B: That's exactly it. Inference forgets as it goes. Training has to remember everything so it can retrace its steps. Push the context to two million tokens and the stuff you're forced to hold onto blows past what the GPUs can physically store.
A: And there's a multiplier here, isn't there — because this is reinforcement learning specifically.
B: [pleased] There is, good catch. The method's GRPO — we spent a whole dive on it a couple of weeks back, so the one line is: instead of one answer per question, you generate a group of answers and grade them against each other. But "a group of answers" means you're now holding several long responses in memory at once, all hanging off the same giant prompt. So the thing that was already too big gets multiplied by your group size.
A: So what's their trick?
B: It's clever, and kind of humble. They notice that the enormous shared prompt is identical for every answer in the group. So instead of pushing that giant prompt through the memory-hungry training machinery once per answer, they run the prompt once, without the training bookkeeping — just far enough to capture the state the answers will need. Then they replay each short answer branch one at a time, and only that little branch carries the full training graph.
A: One expensive shared trunk, computed cheaply once — and then you pay full price only on the small branches, one at a time.
B: That's the picture exactly. You trade time for memory. Replaying one at a time is slower than doing them all at once, if you even could. But memory is the wall you're hitting, not time.
A: And the numbers?
B: On eight GPUs — H-twenty, a mid-tier data-center chip — with a twenty-seven-billion-parameter Qwen model, they push to two-point-one million token positions. And the number that made me sit up: going from a group of two answers to a group of eight adds about a fifth of a gigabyte of peak memory. Almost nothing.
A: [skeptical] Which is where I have to come in, because that number is suspiciously good, and I read the limitations section.
B: You should. Say it.
A: They're honest about this, and it's the whole caveat, so I want it on the record. In their own words — these experiments establish execution capacity rather than complete training correctness. The captured prompt state is detached, and some of the distributed gradient paths remain incomplete.
B: Translate "detached."
A: It means the shared trunk they computed cheaply isn't fully wired into the learning yet. So what they've shown is that you can physically fit a two-million-token reinforcement-learning step onto a fixed set of GPUs without running out of memory. What they have not yet shown is that the whole thing trains correctly end to end and the model actually learns from it. It's a plumbing proof, not a results proof.
B: And I'll completely own that, because it's the right way to read it. This is a systems paper. Nobody here is claiming "we trained a better agent." They're claiming "the memory wall that stopped you from even trying — here's a way through it." Which is the necessary boring step before anyone gets the exciting one.
A: And I'd defend why that's worth a segment. Most of whether a good idea ever happens comes down to whether someone can afford to run it. "Long-context RL is too expensive" is a sentence that has quietly killed a hundred experiments. If this holds up — and it needs the correctness half to hold up too — it moves long-context training from "you need a lab's cluster" toward "you need eight GPUs and some patience."
B: Time instead of money. And time you can wait out. Money you have to have.
A: Which is the whole open-versus-closed story in miniature, again — and I'll leave it right there rather than force it into a theme.
