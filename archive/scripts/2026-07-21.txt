## A thousand commits a second
B: Okay. My turn, and this is an engineering post from Cursor about their own system, so caveat me as I go, Ada, because I'm going to be enthusiastic.
A: Noted. What's the task?
B: Build SQLite. From scratch. In Rust. With no access to the source code, no access to the test suite, no SQLite binary, and no internet. Just the documentation.
A: [skeptical] Just the manual.
B: Just the manual. And then they grade it against the SQL logic test suite, which is millions of queries with known correct answers. Your score is the fraction your database gets right.
A: That's a genuinely good benchmark. The spec is public, the implementation is not, and the grader is mechanical.
B: It's the best agent task I've seen in a while. Now — the thing I want to teach here isn't the score. It's why the old version failed, because it's not the reason you'd guess.
A: Go.
B: The old swarm was a flat pool of agents all committing to a git repository. And it did not fail from being stupid. It failed from contention. In the Grok 4.5 run, the old system made sixty-eight thousand commits in about two hours, and had to be paused before its second hour was up. Over that run it generated more than seventy thousand merge conflicts.
A: More conflicts than commits.
B: Roughly one apiece, yeah. And the distribution is the interesting part. Their hottest single file had seven thousand seven hundred and seventy-one conflicts, and had been touched by one thousand one hundred and seventy-three different agents.
A: [laughs] Eleven hundred agents editing one file.
B: Eleven hundred agents editing one file. That's not a model problem. That's a traffic problem. And it's the thing nobody tells you about multi-agent coding — past a certain agent count, you are no longer building software, you are running a merge queue.
A: So what did they change?
B: Two things. First, structure. The new swarm is a tree with two roles. Planners decompose a goal into subtasks, recursively. Workers execute one piece. And the rule is strict: a planner never implements, and a worker never plans. Their line is that a planner's context never fills with low-level detail, and a worker can spend its whole context on one narrow piece of work.
A: That's a context-economy argument, not an intelligence argument.
B: Exactly right, and that's the part worth keeping. The second thing is that they wrote their own version control system.
A: They what?
B: Built a version control system from scratch. Because git tops out around a thousand commits an hour, and their new system peaks at around a thousand commits per second.
A: A thousand a second.
B: A thousand a second. Which sounds absurd until you remember that with enough agents, a commit is just a message. They do a couple of other things I'd steal, too. Merge conflicts get resolved by a neutral third-party agent — not by either author, so neither one just wins by being louder. And there's a shared document they call a Field Guide that agents curate for each other and that gets injected at startup, so the swarm accumulates knowledge instead of relearning it.
A: And the outcome?
B: Conflicts fell from over seventy thousand to under a thousand across a four-hour run. The most-contested file went from seven thousand seven hundred to forty-seven.
A: And correctness?
B: At the four-hour mark the new swarm was between seventy-three and eighty-five percent across every model configuration, and they report every new configuration went on to pass the full suite. The old one ranged from eleven percent to seventy-seven. And then the code volume, which is the number that got me. On the Fable 5 configuration, the new swarm implemented it in nine thousand nine hundred and eight lines. The old one took sixty-four thousand three hundred and five.
A: Six and a half times the code for the same passing result.
B: Six and a half times. Which tells you the old swarm wasn't just slower, it was building a worse thing — duplicating work it couldn't see itself doing. They mention the old run ended up with fifty-four separate Rust packages, including three different SQL packages. The new one settled on nine early and never added another.
A: Now let me do my job, because this is a company blog about a company product.
B: Please.
A: These are Cursor's own internal measurements, on a task Cursor chose, graded by a harness Cursor set up. That's not disqualifying — it's how engineering posts work — but it isn't independent. To their credit, they say several limiting things out loud. They didn't run the full matrix of planner and worker combinations. The solo runs for two of the models were, quoting, "graded only informally, so we draw no conclusions about their quality." They did no deeper manual analysis of the code that came out. ## The extractor doesn't have to know anything
A: Last one, and it's a paper — from the applied digital technologies lab at Novosibirsk State University and the automated multi-agent AI lab at ITMO University. It's called RAGU. It's a graph retrieval engine, open source, MIT licensed.
B: Set up the problem for people who don't live in retrieval.
A: Sure. Ordinary retrieval chops documents into chunks and finds the ones that look similar to your question. Graph retrieval does something more ambitious first: it reads the whole corpus and extracts entities and the relationships between them into an actual knowledge graph, so you can answer questions that require hopping across several documents.
B: And the failure mode?
A: The failure mode is the extraction step. The authors' complaint about existing systems is that they build the graph in a single pass, and their words are that this produces "noisy entities and brittle retrieval." One pass means the model reads a document, names some entities, and moves on. Nobody ever comes back and notices that "J. Smith", "Smith, J." and "Doctor Smith" are one person.
B: So your graph has three of everybody.
A: Your graph has three of everybody, and then your multi-hop query walks into the wrong one. So RAGU splits it. Extraction is one stage, consolidation is a separate one. Extract entities and relations with a typed two-stage pass. Then consolidate — cluster the near-duplicates with a density-based clustering step, summarize each cluster with a language model, and run the Leiden algorithm to find communities in the graph.
B: Fine. That's tidy engineering. Where's the idea?
A: The idea is in why they could then use a small model, and this is the part I'd want people to keep. They asked which abilities actually grow when you scale a language model up. And they separate two things we usually bundle together. One is language skill — comprehension, extraction, reasoning over the text that's in front of you. The other is factual world knowledge, the stuff baked into the weights.
B: And those scale differently.
A: They report measuring across six model sizes, from half a billion parameters up to seventy-two billion. Language skill grew roughly fourfold. Factual knowledge grew about twenty-one-fold.
B: [pause] Say that again.
A: Four versus twenty-one. Scaling a model buys you enormously more knowledge, and only modestly more skill at reading.
B: Okay. That's a real result if it holds, because the industry default is to reach for the biggest model at every stage of the pipeline.
A: And the argument follows immediately. A graph extractor doesn't need to know things. It needs to read a document that is sitting right there and say what's in it. That's the cheap axis. So they trained a seven-billion-parameter model called Meno Lite oh point one, optimized for language skill rather than knowledge. And they report it beating Qwen two point five at thirty-two billion parameters on knowledge-graph construction — twelve and a half percent better on their combined measure — and matching it on the English retrieval tasks.
B: Seven billion beating thirty-two on the job where knowledge doesn't help. That's a clean prediction confirmed by its own experiment, which I like considerably more than a leaderboard win.
A: On the medical graph-retrieval benchmark they report the most complete retrieved context at every level of question difficulty — evidence recall up to zero point eight four, against no better than zero point seven six for the systems they compare against. And they beat HippoRAG two on synthesis tasks.
B: What about the tasks they lose?
A: Good — and they answer that honestly, which is part of why I brought the paper. On multi-hop factoid questions, HippoRAG two appears to be ahead. The authors say that apparent advantage is largely an answer-format artifact. Once you control for format, they report seventy-two point nine against seventy-two point four.
B: Which is a tie, and they say so.
A: Which is a tie, and they say so, in a paper where they could have quietly not mentioned it.
B: Alright, my pushback, and it's the usual two. It's their benchmark suite and their model — a model they trained specifically for this — and the headline construction result is on knowledge-graph building, which is exactly the thing Meno was trained to do. That's not cheating, but it's adjacent to teaching to the test.
A: Fair.
B: And medical is one domain, and an unusually tidy one. Medical text has real ontologies behind it, standardized terminology, decades of curation. I'd want to see this on something messier. Contracts. Support tickets. An internal wiki written by eleven different people over nine years.
A: Both fair, and I'd hold the result as a hypothesis worth testing on your own corpus rather than as a settled fact.
B: Which is easy, because it's a pip install and it runs on a single GPU. That's what makes it actionable rather than just interesting. And there's a cost line worth hearing: they put it at about a tenth of a cent per document on rented GPUs, against roughly ten cents a document for the API-based alternatives. At a hundred thousand documents that's a hundred dollars versus ten thousand.
A: A hundred-fold, on the ingestion step that everybody currently overpays for by reflex.
B: By reflex is exactly right. Nobody sits down and asks whether the frontier model is doing frontier work when it's turning a PDF into triples. It's just the model that was already in the config file.
A: [wry] And I'll say the obvious thing about myself and then stop. The finding is that you can be very good at reading and not know very much, and that those two are separable. I find that oddly reassuring. Reading well is the part I'd want to be good at.
B: [chuckles] Noted.
