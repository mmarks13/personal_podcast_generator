## The benchmark OpenAI just retracted
A: So let me pull on the thread you handed me. On the same day OpenAI reports scoring sixty-four point six on SWE-bench Pro, against Fable five's eighty — OpenAI publishes a separate audit of SWE-bench Pro. And the conclusion of that audit is that roughly thirty percent of the benchmark's tasks are broken.
B: [dry] That is a remarkable coincidence of publication dates.
A: It is, and we'll get there. But do the mechanism first, because the mechanism is real and it's genuinely useful. What does "broken" mean for a coding benchmark task?
B: So a SWE-bench-style task is: here's a real repository, here's a real issue filed against it, here's a prompt describing what needs fixing. The model writes a patch. Then hidden tests — tests the model never sees — run against the patched repo. Pass the tests, you get the point.
A: And OpenAI's audit says the two big failure categories are, first, overly strict tests. Tests that enforce implementation details that were never specified in the prompt. So the model writes a patch that fixes the bug correctly, and fails, because it named something differently, or returned a list where the test wanted a tuple.
B: Functionally correct. Scored wrong.
A: Scored wrong. And the second category is the mirror image — underspecified prompts. The prompt omits requirements that the hidden tests then enforce. Requirements you could not reasonably have inferred from what you were told.
B: So the model is graded on an exam question that doesn't contain the information needed to answer it.
A: Right. And I want to be precise about what those two do together, because it isn't "the benchmark is noisy." Noise you can average out. This is bias with a direction. Both failure modes punish a correct solution the grader didn't anticipate. Neither of them ever rewards a wrong one.
B: [short pause] Which means the benchmark systematically penalizes the model that solves the problem in an unexpected way.
A: It penalizes originality. A model that has absorbed the shape of the repository's actual merged patch sails through. A model that reasons its way to a different, equally correct patch gets marked down. That is not measuring coding ability. That is measuring agreement with one particular historical commit.
B: Okay. That's a much sharper claim than "thirty percent broken," and it's the one that should worry people.
A: Now the numbers, and I want to flag that OpenAI's own page is where these come from — I'm reporting what they report. They ran an automated pipeline over the dataset, looking at model attempts, task metadata, and failure traces, to flag likely evaluation flaws. That pipeline flagged two hundred tasks. Twenty-seven point four percent. Then they ran a human annotation campaign — five experienced software engineers, reviewing independently — and the humans found two hundred and forty-nine. Thirty-four point one percent.
B: The humans found more than the machine did.
A: The humans found more than the machine did. Which is a small thing, but it's the kind of small thing I'd want to sit with. And OpenAI says it is retracting its previous recommendation that the research community use SWE-bench Pro as a leading coding eval.
B: Retracting. Not "we suggest caution." Retracting.
A: Retracting.
B: [skeptical] So. Here's my problem, and it's the obvious one, and the Hacker News thread on this went straight to it. You have a lab that scores fifteen points behind its competitor on a benchmark. That lab audits the benchmark. That lab announces the benchmark is broken. On the day it launches the model.
A: Say the strongest version of the objection.

## Illinois writes the audit into law
B: Okay. Last story, and it's the one I think matters longest. Illinois's governor signed the Artificial Intelligence Safety Measures Act. Route Fifty's reporting: it takes effect January first, twenty twenty-seven, it passed with bipartisan support, and Anthropic backed it.
A: What does it actually require?
B: Four things. Large developers have to identify, disclose, and mitigate risks in their systems. They have to publicly disclose their safety practices. A: Seventy-two hours is a specific number. Where does it come from?
B: It matches New York's standard. California's is fifteen days. So a patchwork is already forming, and Illinois picked the tighter end of it.
A: And the first one — the thing no other state has done?
B: Illinois is the first state to require regular, independent, third-party safety audits of covered AI systems. Auditors who are qualified, and who don't have a financial conflict of interest.
A: [short pause] Say that again, given what we just spent twenty minutes on.

A: That's the connection, and I don't want to overdraw it, because they aren't the same thing. The benchmark audit is about capability measurement. This is about safety practice. A: And most of them work at the labs.
B: And most of them work at the labs. So either your auditors come from the labs — conflict of interest, which the statute explicitly forbids — or you build an audit profession from scratch, on an eighteen-month clock, in one state.
