## Two million tokens on eight GPUs
A: All right. Mine is a systems paper, and I want to walk it properly, because the idea underneath outlives the paper.
B: Go.
A: It's called LongStraw, out of Mind Lab Research, posted July sixteenth. About two hundred upvotes on Hugging Face, which is why it surfaced this week rather than the day it landed. And it opens on a gap I don't think gets stated often enough. The authors write that a widening gap separates million-token inference from reinforcement-learning post-training, which remains at two hundred fifty-six thousand tokens or below.
B: Wait. Say that in practical terms.
A: You can run a model over a million tokens of context. You mostly cannot train one with reinforcement learning at that length. So a model gets its long-context behavior at deployment by generalizing out from much shorter training.
B: Which for agents is the whole ballgame. My agent's context isn't a long document, it's a trajectory — tool outputs, observations, a file it read four hours ago. That's accumulated history.
A: And that's precisely the case the authors name. So: why is training so much harder than inference here? This is the teachable part.
B: Please.
A: In inference you carry one sequence forward and you're done with each token once you've passed it. In reinforcement learning with group-relative policy optimization — the family that's eaten post-training over the last couple of years — you take one history, generate several responses from it, score them against each other, and backpropagate. The paper's framing is that attention and long-lived backward state become the primary GPU memory barrier.
B: Because backpropagation needs the forward pass kept around.
A: That's the classical trap, and it's an old one. To compute gradients you need the intermediate activations from the forward pass. So memory scales with everything you did on the way forward. Now multiply that by several responses all hanging off the same shared history.
B: So it's not that the context is long. It's that the context is long and there are several futures branching off it, every one of them needing gradients.
A: Exactly that. And the fix has a lineage — it's the same instinct as gradient checkpointing, which the field has leaned on for years: don't store the intermediate results, store a few checkpoints and recompute the rest when you need them. Trade arithmetic for memory. Compute is comparatively cheap. Memory is a wall at a fixed address.
B: So LongStraw is checkpointing.
A: LongStraw is that instinct, applied with a much sharper sense of what's actually shared. Their mechanism, and their own words are precise here: resident state retains only model-native prompt state needed by later tokens — not the full graph. So the enormous shared prompt is kept, but in the model's own compact form, not as a differentiable computation graph.
B: And then?
A: Then, quoting: response replay restores that state, scores graph-free old and reference branches, rebuilds one policy response under autograd, backpropagates, and pops to the prompt boundary.
B: [slowly] Okay. So the branches you only need a score from — those run with no gradient machinery at all.
A: None. Those are cheap.
B: And the one branch you're actually training on gets rebuilt with the full apparatus, gets its gradient, and then you throw the apparatus away and go back to the prompt.
A: And repeat. One response at a time, off a prompt you never rebuild. The insight underneath is economic: in this objective the expensive thing is shared and the cheap thing is what varies. So keep the shared thing resident, and replay the varying thing.
B: That's clean. That's actually clean. What did it buy them?
A: On eight H20 GPUs, with a twenty-seven-billion-parameter Qwen model: exact-attention, response-only training at two million ninety-seven thousand token positions. And a prefix of about four point four million positions supporting eight cycles — sixty-four replays — at roughly eighty-four gigabytes per rank.
B: Eight GPUs. Not eight hundred.
A: Eight. They also report that group size barely costs anything: about zero point two one gigabytes of additional peak memory per expansion of the group.
B: That's the number that would change my planning, honestly. Group size is normally exactly where you get punished.
A: They also ran GLM five point two across thirty-two of the same GPUs — deterministic execution at two million positions, and two full backward passes through all seventy-eight layers.
B: Right. Now let me do my job, because there are two sentences in that abstract a headline will absolutely eat.
A: Do it.
B: First — and I appreciate that they wrote this themselves — the measured objective is response-only execution, not full-sequence gradient equivalence. Which means this is not arithmetically identical to training the whole thing the ordinary way, and they are not claiming it is.
A: They aren't. That's a meaningful asterisk.
B: Second, they describe the integration with a real training loop as preliminary validation. So what we have is an execution stack that demonstrably holds two million positions in memory on a modest number of GPUs. What we do not have is a model that came out the other side better at anything.
A: No task results.
B: No task results. And to be fair, that's not a flaw, it's a scope — this is a systems paper and it's honest about being one. But "two million token reinforcement learning" is going to get repeated this week as though somebody trained an agent at two million tokens, and nobody did.
A: [short pause] Agreed. Though the reason to care isn't the record. It's the diagnosis in their closing line: that practical training context is set by resident-state lifetime, replay and distributed ownership, rather than attention kernels alone.
B: Meaning everyone's been optimizing the wrong layer.
A: Meaning the field has spent years making attention cheaper — and the ceiling on training length may sit somewhere else entirely, in how long you hold state and who owns it. If that's right, a long line of work has been polishing a surface that wasn't the binding constraint.
B: That I'll take. Even without a benchmark.

## Negotiating with the model

B: [short pause] Negotiating.
A: Negotiating. And Chris Anley, chief scientist at NCC Group, gives the reason it can't be cleanly fixed: the same operation is offence and defence simultaneously.
