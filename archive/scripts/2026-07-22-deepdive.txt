## The question vector search can't answer
A: Start with what ordinary retrieval-augmented generation does, because GraphRAG is defined against it. You take your documents, chop them into chunks — a few hundred tokens each — and run every chunk through an embedding model, which turns it into a vector, a long list of numbers. A question comes in, you embed the question the same way, and you find the chunks whose vectors sit closest to it. Those go in the prompt.
B: And that works. I want to say that up front, because there's a whole genre of graph-retrieval content that opens by pretending vector search is broken. It isn't. It's fast, it's cheap, it's one dependency.
A: It's excellent, and it has one structural blind spot. It answers questions of the form: which passage looks like this question. So ask it what the third-quarter filing said about restructuring costs, and it's perfect — that sentence lives in a chunk somewhere, and the chunk resembles the question.
B: Now ask it something else.
A: Now ask it: what are the main themes across this entire collection. There is no chunk that resembles that question. The answer isn't hiding in one passage — it's distributed across two thousand of them. So similarity search retrieves your top ten chunks, which are ten fairly arbitrary passages that happen to use the words "main" and "theme," and the model dutifully summarizes those ten.
B: And you get an answer that reads fine and is essentially a random sample.
A: Confidently wrong in a way that is very hard to notice. The Microsoft Research team that published GraphRAG framed this precisely: that kind of question isn't a retrieval task at all, it's query-focused summarization. You're asking for a summary of a corpus, oriented around a question. And the classical methods for that don't scale to the amount of text a modern retrieval system indexes.
B: So the pitch is: build something at index time that a similarity search can't give you.
A: That's the whole idea. Do expensive work once, up front, so that global questions have something to retrieve against.
## Building the graph: what indexing actually does
A: Four stages. And I want to walk them properly, because every one of them has a failure mode that shows up later.
B: Go.
A: Stage one, you chunk the text — same as ordinary retrieval. But the chunk size choice matters differently here, and the paper is unusually candid about it. They report that with six-hundred-token chunks, the model extracted almost twice as many entity references as it did with twenty-four-hundred-token chunks.
B: Twice as many, from the same text? Why?
A: Because the model has to hold the whole chunk and enumerate everything in it, and attention over a long chunk goes soft in the middle. It's the recall-versus-cost tradeoff, just relocated. Smaller chunks, more thorough extraction, many more model calls.
B: [wry] So the very first knob is "how much money would you like to spend on completeness." Great start.
A: Stage two is the interesting one. For every chunk, a language model is prompted to extract entities — people, organizations, concepts, each with a type and a short description — and then the relationships between them, also with a description. Not a bare triple like "Ada, works-at, MIT." A described relationship: who these two are to each other, in a sentence.
B: Where does that go wrong?
A: Two places, and we touched the first one yesterday. The model reads a document, names "J. Smith," moves on. Another chunk says "Doctor Smith." Nobody comes back to notice those are one person, so your graph has three of everybody, and a multi-hop query walks into the wrong copy. That's entity resolution, and it is the single most under-discussed problem in this entire field.
B: And the second?
A: The second is that a single pass simply misses things. The original paper's answer is a technique they call gleanings — you ask the model whether it missed any entities, it says yes or no, and if yes you ask again, with the previous extraction still in context. Multiple rounds of that.
B: So you're paying for the same chunk three or four times.
A: You are. And it measurably helps — the entity counts climb with each round.
B: Okay, hold on. I want to name something here, because it's the thing that would actually worry me in production. Everything downstream of this step is built on the output of a language model that was asked to be thorough. If the extraction is wrong, nothing later in the pipeline can tell.
A: Nothing later can tell. The graph is not a fact-checked object. It is a model's reading of the corpus, frozen and made queryable.
B: That's the sentence I'd put on the whiteboard.
A: Stage three: you merge. All the instances of the same entity across all chunks get collapsed into one node, and the model summarizes their descriptions into a single description. Same for relationships — one edge, one summary, and the number of source chunks it came from becomes the edge weight. Now you have a real graph. Nodes are entities, edges are relationships, and both carry model-written summaries.
B: And it's still just a graph at this point. I could query it directly.
A: You could, and some systems stop right here. GraphRAG doesn't, because a graph still doesn't answer a global question — it's a pile of local facts, just better organized. So stage four: community detection.
B: This is the Leiden part.
A: This is the Leiden part. Leiden is a community detection algorithm — you hand it a weighted graph and it partitions the nodes into groups that are densely connected on the inside and sparsely connected to everything else. Clusters of things that keep coming up together. And GraphRAG runs it hierarchically: find the communities, then recursively find sub-communities inside each one, down to leaf communities that can't be partitioned any further.
B: So you get a tree. Coarse themes at the top, tight little clusters at the bottom.
A: And here is the payoff. For every community, at every level, you generate a summary — a model-written report on what this cluster of entities and relationships is about. Bottom-up: leaf communities are summarized from their nodes and edges directly, prioritized by node degree until you hit a token limit, and the higher communities are summarized from their children's summaries.
B: [interested] Oh. So the top of the tree is a summary of summaries of summaries.
A: The top of the tree is a description of what the entire corpus is about, assembled from below rather than guessed from above. That is the artifact. That's the thing that didn't exist before you paid for indexing.
## Asking it a question
B: Fine. I've paid the bill. What happens when I type a question?
A: Two modes, and they're genuinely different. Local search is roughly what you'd expect — find the entities the question mentions, pull in their neighborhood in the graph, their descriptions, their relationships, the source chunks behind them, and put all of that in the prompt.
B: That's vector search with extra steps and a hop.
A: That's fair, and we're going to come back to whether the extra steps pay for themselves. Global search is the mode that justifies the architecture. You pick a level of the community hierarchy. Then you take every community summary at that level — all of them — shuffle them, and split them into batches that fit a context window.
B: All of them. Not the relevant ones.
A: All of them. That's the point. Nothing is filtered out by similarity, so nothing is missed because it failed to look like the question. Then, in parallel, the model answers your question against each batch independently. That's the map step. And each partial answer comes back with a self-assigned helpfulness score, zero to one hundred, for how useful that partial answer is to your question.
B: The model grades its own homework.
A: The model grades its own homework, yes, and you should hold on to that skepticism. Then the reduce step: sort the partial answers by that score, descending, and pour them into a fresh context window until it's full. Whatever fits gets synthesized into the final answer.
B: So the score isn't really a quality judgment. It's a ranking for what survives the context budget.
A: That is exactly what it is. It's triage.
B: Right, now the number I actually care about. What does it cost to answer?
A: This surprised me. On their podcast-transcript dataset — about a million tokens of source material — answering from the root-level community summaries took roughly twenty-six thousand tokens of context.
B: Out of a million.
A: Out of a million. Versus the condition where you stuff the entire source text in, which is the full million. So a couple of percent of the corpus, at the top level.
B: [surprised] Huh. That's the part I didn't expect. I'd assumed the graph made queries more expensive, not less.
A: The graph makes indexing dramatically more expensive and queries cheaper. The whole design is amortization — you pre-pay, once, so that global questions become tractable at all. Which is fine if you run a lot of queries against a stable corpus, and it's terrible if your corpus changes hourly.
B: Because a changing corpus means re-extracting, re-merging, re-clustering, re-summarizing.
A: And the community structure can shift globally from a purely local change. That's a real operational problem, and it's why several later systems — LightRAG among them — put incremental update at the center of their design rather than treating it as an afterthought.
## The other family: retrieval as a random walk
A: I want to spend a few minutes on a different lineage, because "GraphRAG" gets used as one word and it's really two ideas wearing one name.
B: This is the part where you tell me it's older than I think.
A: [amused] It's older than you think. The Microsoft approach builds a graph so that it can summarize. The other approach builds a graph so that it can walk it. And the cleanest example is a system called HippoRAG, whose framing is explicitly neuroscientific — the hippocampal indexing theory of human long-term memory. The idea being that the cortex stores the content, and the hippocampus stores a sparse index that lets you pattern-complete: one cue, and the whole associated memory comes back.
B: How does that cash out as an algorithm?
A: You build the knowledge graph the same way — entities and relations extracted by a model. But at query time, instead of summarizing communities, you find the entities the question mentions, drop probability mass on those nodes, and run Personalized PageRank.
B: PageRank. As in the search engine.
A: As in nineteen ninety-eight. The original PageRank asks: if you randomly walked this graph forever, following links, where would you spend your time? Personalized PageRank adds one twist — every so often the walker teleports back, not to a random page, but to your starting set. So the ranking becomes "important, relative to these specific nodes."
B: So the question's entities are the seed, the walk spreads outward through relationships, and whatever accumulates weight is what you retrieve.
A: And multi-hop falls out for free. You never explicitly plan a two-hop query. The walk goes two hops because that's what walks do. One retrieval pass instead of an iterative retrieve-reason-retrieve loop — the authors report that's something like ten to thirty times cheaper and six to thirteen times faster than the iterative methods at comparable quality, with up to twenty percent improvement on multi-hop question answering over the prior state of the art.
B: That's a much more appealing shape to me as a builder. No pre-summarization bill sitting there before I've served a single query.
A: And notice what it buys and what it doesn't. Personalized PageRank is superb at "what connects to what" — the associative question. It gives you nothing at all on "what are the themes," because a random walk has no notion of the whole. The two families genuinely solve different problems, and they got the same name.
B: Which is, I suspect, most of why the discourse around this is a mess.
A: Most of it.
## What the evidence actually says
B: [skeptical] All right. This is where I get to be difficult. Because I've read the vendor comparisons — the ones claiming eighty-six percent versus thirty-two percent on multi-hop, vector search dropping to literally zero accuracy — and those numbers smell.
A: They should smell. So let's use the careful work instead. There's a systematic evaluation that put four different families side by side — knowledge-graph retrieval, community-based GraphRAG, the text-centric graph-guided systems like the HippoRAG line, and hierarchical summarization — across single-hop, multi-hop, and summarization tasks.
B: Give me the headline.
A: On Natural Questions — single-hop, factual — plain retrieval scored about sixty-four point eight on their F-one metric. Community GraphRAG's local mode scored sixty-three point zero. Plain retrieval wins.
B: On the easy case. Fine, that's expected. Multi-hop?
A: On the multi-hop benchmark, plain retrieval got about sixty-seven percent accuracy. The best graph method got about seventy point three.
B: [flat] Three points.
A: Three points.
B: Now tell me the indexing time on that same dataset.
A: Ordinary retrieval built its index in one hundred thirty-five seconds. The graph methods took between five thousand five hundred and seven thousand seven hundred seconds.
B: So call it forty to fifty-seven times the build time, for three points of accuracy. [short pause] I want to be careful not to overstate this, but that is not the trade the marketing describes.
A: It is not. And there's a finding underneath it that I think is the most important number in this whole episode. They checked how many of the answer entities — the things you would actually need in order to answer the questions — even made it into the constructed knowledge graph.
B: And?
A: About sixty-five point eight percent on one dataset. Sixty-five point five on the other.
B: A third of the answers aren't in the graph at all.
A: A third of the answers aren't in the graph at all. Which closes the loop on what you said ten minutes ago: the graph is a model's reading of the corpus, and a model's reading is lossy. You can have flawless retrieval over an index that is missing a third of what you need.
B: And that's the failure mode I would never catch in a demo. Because the demo questions are, by construction, about the entities that did get extracted.
A: They always are.
B: I want to add one more caution, because it applies to the original results too. Those comprehensiveness and diversity win rates — GraphRAG beating vector retrieval something like seventy-two to eighty-three percent of the time on comprehensiveness — those are head-to-head judgments made by a language model.
A: They are. And the evaluation paper flags this directly: judge-based evaluation can be highly sensitive to presentation order. Which answer goes first can move the verdict.
B: So the strongest evidence for the whole approach is measured with an instrument we know is bent.
A: I'd put it slightly less harshly. The comprehensiveness win rates were large and consistent across both of their datasets, which is more than order effects alone would produce. The diversity results were shakier — clearly weaker on the news corpus than on the podcast one. But your point stands: the headline numbers come from a judge, not from ground truth.
B: And where the tasks do have ground truth, the margin collapses to three points.
A: That's the honest summary, yes.
## The cost cliff
B: So does anybody fix the economics, or is the conclusion just "don't"?
A: The economics moved, and moved fast. The same lab published a follow-up called LazyGraphRAG, and the design principle is right there in the name — defer everything you possibly can.
B: Defer what, exactly?
A: All of it. No language model anywhere in the indexing pipeline. Instead of prompting a model for entities, you use plain natural-language-processing noun phrase extraction — cheap, deterministic, decades old — to pull out concepts, and you connect concepts that co-occur. Then graph statistics find the community structure. No model-written entity descriptions, no pre-generated community summaries, nothing.
B: So the index is nearly free and the graph is much dumber.
A: Much dumber. And the intelligence gets spent at query time instead, only on the part of the corpus the query actually touches. It combines a best-first search — the parts most similar to your question — with a breadth-first sweep through the community structure, deepening iteratively into the sub-communities that turn out to be relevant.
B: What's the knob?
A: A relevance test budget. One number: how many relevance checks you're willing to pay for on this query. That is the entire cost-quality dial.
B: [approving] Okay, that I like. That's a knob I can put in a config file and defend in a design review.
A: The reported numbers: indexing costs identical to vector retrieval, and one tenth of one percent of the cost of full GraphRAG. And on global queries, comparable answer quality to GraphRAG's global search at more than seven hundred times lower query cost.
B: Seven hundred.
A: Seven hundred. Now — same caveat, that is the authors evaluating their own system, with model-judged comparisons, on synthetic queries. But the architectural argument doesn't depend on the judge at all. Pre-summarizing an entire corpus means paying to summarize the ninety-nine percent of it that nobody is ever going to ask about.
B: Which is the lesson from every cache I have ever built. Eager is elegant, lazy is affordable.
## So what should you actually do
B: Give me the decision rule. Somebody's listening on a walk and has to choose something on Monday.
A: Start from the shape of the question, not from the technology. If your users ask what the contract says about termination — local, factual, the answer lives in a passage — use vector retrieval, and don't let anyone talk you out of it.
B: And if it's "what are the recurring failure patterns across three years of incident reports"?
A: That's the query-focused summarization case, and it's the one where nothing you do to a similarity search will help you. That's where the community-summary architecture is solving your actual problem rather than decorating it.
B: And the associative case — how is this supplier connected to that regulatory filing?
A: The walk-the-graph family. Personalized PageRank over an extracted graph.
B: My addition, from the builder's chair: measure your query mix before you build anything at all. My strong suspicion is that most teams reaching for graph retrieval have a query distribution that is ninety percent local, and they're paying a forty-times indexing bill to improve the other ten percent by three points.
A: And measure your entity coverage. That sixty-five percent figure is not a property of graph retrieval in general — it's a property of one extraction pipeline on one corpus, and yours will be different. But you can check it. Take fifty questions you genuinely care about, and ask whether the entities in their answers exist as nodes.
B: That's half a day of work that will save you a quarter.
A: Now, speculating, and I'm flagging it clearly: where I think this goes is agent memory. An agent accumulating months of interactions has exactly the two problems this literature has been chewing on. It needs the associative jump, and it needs the global picture — what have I learned about this person — and neither one of those is a similarity search.
B: With the caveat that the memory-system evaluations we covered on the daily last month found graph memory strong on factual recall and weak on anything time-dependent. Graphs are famously bad at "and then it changed."
A: Which is a real, unsolved gap, and I'd watch that gap more closely than I'd watch any benchmark number.
## Wrap
B: Where I land: the mechanism is genuinely clever, and the marketing around it is genuinely not. Build the graph when your questions are global or associative, measure before you commit, and stay honest that what you're indexing is a model's opinion of your documents.
A: Where I land: the interesting thing GraphRAG proved isn't graphs. It's that you can spend compute at index time to create structure that was never in the text, and then retrieve against the structure instead of the text. The graph is one way to do that. It won't be the last.
