Amazon retires the Turk

Does the AI tutor actually work

Alan: Second one. There's a paper that hit Hacker News hard this weekend, out of Dartmouth, and it's one of the few AI-in-education studies with real exam numbers attached — not a survey, not vibes. Alan: Introductory Statistics at Dartmouth — the one non-math-majors take. Three sections, about a hundred and fifty students, this past spring. Alan: You went straight for the throat, and it's exactly the right question — and to their credit, so do the authors. This is an observational study. No random assignment. They name self-selection as the central threat, in those words.

Ada: A floor of zero-point-seven is still large for education, I'll grant that. But I want the mechanism, because "AI grades your homework and you learn more" isn't a mechanism. It's a correlation wearing a hopeful label.

Alan: And this is the part I loved, because the study accidentally ran its own controlled experiment. Partway through, students complained the written-answer grader was too harsh. So for one module, they switched to multiple-choice only. Then later switched back.

Ada: Oh — that's a natural experiment handed to them on a plate.

Alan: Right. And the result is stark. In the modules with written, constructed-response questions — where you have to produce the answer out of your own head — more practice tracked higher exam scores. In the multiple-choice-only module, that relationship completely vanished. Same platform, same kind of engaged students, zero dosage effect the moment the questions became "pick A, B, or C."

Ada: Huh. Now that's a mechanism — and notice it's not about the AI at all. That's the testing effect. The doer effect, in the education literature. The act of retrieving an answer, of generating it yourself, is what lays down the memory. Recognizing the right option in a list does almost nothing by comparison. And that's not new — that result goes back decades.

Alan: Which reframes the whole story. The reason this worked isn't that a language model is a magic tutor. It's that a language model is finally cheap enough to grade open-ended answers at scale — so you can make a few thousand students write instead of click. Alan: So the honest takeaway for anyone building education tools: the win isn't the tutor that talks. It's the assessment that makes the student generate. Three tricks, one dial

Ada: Last real one, and it's for the people who train these models — but stay with me, because the idea underneath is elegant and you don't need any of the math. There's a paper going around whose title is basically the whole abstract: "GRPO, Dr. GRPO, and DAPO are three operations on one number."

Alan: Those are three reinforcement-learning methods. Three recipes for teaching a model to reason.

Ada: Right — the family of techniques behind a lot of this year's reasoning models. And in the field they're treated as three rival tricks, three separate papers, and teams genuinely agonize over which to use. This paper proves they're the same trick, with one knob set three different ways.

Alan: Okay. Teach me. What's the one number?

Ada: The setup is simpler than the acronyms make it sound. To train one of these models, you take a problem, and you have the model answer it many times — say, sixteen attempts. An automatic checker marks each attempt right or wrong. So now you've got a group of sixteen answers, some right, some wrong. And the question is: how much should the model learn from this one problem?

Alan: And the naive intuition is — reinforce the ones it got right, push away from the ones it got wrong.

Ada: But look at the group as a whole. Take the two extremes. If all sixteen come back right — the model already knows this cold. Nothing to teach. If all sixteen come back wrong — it has no toehold, no correct example to pull toward. Also nothing to teach.

Alan: So the sweet spot's in the middle. Eight right, eight wrong.

Ada: Maximum disagreement — the problem the model is right on the knife's edge of, where a nudge actually moves it. And here's the identity at the heart of the paper. For right-or-wrong grading, the standard deviation of those marks — the statistician's measure of how much the group disagrees — comes out to a clean little formula. Biggest when the group splits evenly. Exactly zero when they all agree.

Alan: So disagreement is the whole signal. High when it's a coin flip, zero when it's unanimous.

Ada: And the punchline is that this disagreement number isn't some side-calculation bolted onto the training. For these binary rewards, it is the size of the learning update. A split group teaches the most. A unanimous group teaches nothing — the authors' own phrase is that it "falls silent."

Alan: And so the three methods —

Ada: — are just three ways of handling that one number. The original one, GRPO, divides by it. The second, Dr. GRPO — GRPO "done right" — says stop dividing by it. And the third, DAPO, says throw away the groups where it's zero, because a silent group is wasted compute anyway. Three papers. One dial.

Alan: Alright, let me push, though — because "it's all secretly the same" papers can be a little too pleased with themselves. Does this change anything I'd actually do? Or is it a lovely unification that leaves my job exactly where it was?

Ada: Fair, and that's the part I'd defend — it's not just aesthetics. Once you know the update size is literally that formula, you can compute things you used to eyeball. Like: how many times do I need to sample each problem before the signal is trustworthy? And what fraction of my groups are going to come back unanimous and teach nothing — so I know how much compute I'm about to burn for free? Both of those fall straight out of the identity, as closed forms. And they check it on a big real-world math dataset and a controlled training run.

Alan: So it turns three folk remedies into one thing you can actually reason about. That I'll take. The knob was always there — they just proved it's one knob, and told you what it's wired to.

Ada: And historically, that's the satisfying kind of result. Not a new capability — a clarification. Somebody noticed three things everyone treated as separate were one object, seen from three angles. The field does that far too rarely.
