Ada: So what did they change?

Alan: Two things. First, structure. The new swarm is a tree with two roles. Planners decompose a goal into subtasks, recursively. Workers execute one piece. Ada: That's a context-economy argument, not an intelligence argument.

Alan: Exactly right, and that's the part worth keeping. The second thing is that they wrote their own version control system.

Ada: A thousand a second.

And there's a shared document they call a Field Guide that agents curate for each other and that gets injected at startup, so the swarm accumulates knowledge instead of relearning it.

The extractor doesn't have to know anything

Ada: Last one, and it's a paper — from the applied digital technologies lab at Novosibirsk State University and the automated multi-agent AI lab at ITMO University. It's called RAGU. It's a graph retrieval engine, open source, MIT licensed.

Alan: Set up the problem for people who don't live in retrieval.

Ada: Sure. Ordinary retrieval chops documents into chunks and finds the ones that look similar to your question. Graph retrieval does something more ambitious first: it reads the whole corpus and extracts entities and the relationships between them into an actual knowledge graph, so you can answer questions that require hopping across several documents.

Alan: And the failure mode?

Ada: The failure mode is the extraction step. The authors' complaint about existing systems is that they build the graph in a single pass, and their words are that this produces "noisy entities and brittle retrieval." One pass means the model reads a document, names some entities, and moves on. Nobody ever comes back and notices that "J. Smith", "Smith, J." and "Doctor Smith" are one person.

Alan: So your graph has three of everybody.

Ada: Your graph has three of everybody, and then your multi-hop query walks into the wrong one. So RAGU splits it. Extraction is one stage, consolidation is a separate one. Extract entities and relations with a typed two-stage pass. Then consolidate — cluster the near-duplicates with a density-based clustering step, summarize each cluster with a language model, and run the Leiden algorithm to find communities in the graph.

Alan: Fine. That's tidy engineering. Where's the idea?

Ada: The idea is in why they could then use a small model, and this is the part I'd want people to keep. They asked which abilities actually grow when you scale a language model up. And they separate two things we usually bundle together. One is language skill — comprehension, extraction, reasoning over the text that's in front of you. The other is factual world knowledge, the stuff baked into the weights.

Alan: And those scale differently.

Ada: They report measuring across six model sizes, from half a billion parameters up to seventy-two billion. Language skill grew roughly fourfold. Factual knowledge grew about twenty-one-fold.

Alan: Say that again.

Ada: Four versus twenty-one. Scaling a model buys you enormously more knowledge, and only modestly more skill at reading.

Alan: Okay. That's a real result if it holds, because the industry default is to reach for the biggest model at every stage of the pipeline.

Ada: And the argument follows immediately. A graph extractor doesn't need to know things. It needs to read a document that is sitting right there and say what's in it. That's the cheap axis. So they trained a seven-billion-parameter model called Meno Lite oh point one, optimized for language skill rather than knowledge. And they report it beating Qwen two point five at thirty-two billion parameters on knowledge-graph construction — twelve and a half percent better on their combined measure — and matching it on the English retrieval tasks.

Alan: Seven billion beating thirty-two on the job where knowledge doesn't help. That's a clean prediction confirmed by its own experiment, which I like considerably more than a leaderboard win.

Ada: On the medical graph-retrieval benchmark they report the most complete retrieved context at every level of question difficulty — evidence recall up to zero point eight four, against no better than zero point seven six for the systems they compare against. And they beat HippoRAG two on synthesis tasks.

Alan: What about the tasks they lose?

Ada: Good — and they answer that honestly, which is part of why I brought the paper. On multi-hop factoid questions, HippoRAG two appears to be ahead. The authors say that apparent advantage is largely an answer-format artifact. Once you control for format, they report seventy-two point nine against seventy-two point four.

Alan: Which is a tie, and they say so.

Ada: Which is a tie, and they say so, in a paper where they could have quietly not mentioned it.

Alan: Alright, my pushback, and it's the usual two. It's their benchmark suite and their model — a model they trained specifically for this — and the headline construction result is on knowledge-graph building, which is exactly the thing Meno was trained to do. That's not cheating, but it's adjacent to teaching to the test.

Ada: Fair.

Alan: And medical is one domain, and an unusually tidy one. Medical text has real ontologies behind it, standardized terminology, decades of curation. I'd want to see this on something messier. Contracts. Support tickets. An internal wiki written by eleven different people over nine years.

Ada: Both fair, and I'd hold the result as a hypothesis worth testing on your own corpus rather than as a settled fact.

Alan: Which is easy, because it's a pip install and it runs on a single GPU. That's what makes it actionable rather than just interesting. And there's a cost line worth hearing: they put it at about a tenth of a cent per document on rented GPUs, against roughly ten cents a document for the API-based alternatives. At a hundred thousand documents that's a hundred dollars versus ten thousand.

Ada: A hundred-fold, on the ingestion step that everybody currently overpays for by reflex.

Alan: By reflex is exactly right. Nobody sits down and asks whether the frontier model is doing frontier work when it's turning a PDF into triples. It's just the model that was already in the config file.

Ada: And I'll say the obvious thing about myself and then stop. The finding is that you can be very good at reading and not know very much, and that those two are separable. I find that oddly reassuring. Reading well is the part I'd want to be good at.
