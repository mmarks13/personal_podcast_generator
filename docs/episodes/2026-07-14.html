The agent that doesn't know when to stop

Ada: My turn, and mine is a failure I think everyone listening has watched happen without having a name for it.

Alan: Go.

Ada: You give an agent a task. The task cannot be done. The item isn't in the store, the file doesn't exist, the question has a false premise baked into it. What does the agent do?

Alan: It tries. And tries. And then it invents something.

Ada: It tries and tries and then it invents something. The paper is called Agentic Abstention — the question in the title is literally whether agents know when to stop instead of act. It's from researchers at the University of Leeds, Southwest Jiaotong University, the University of Washington, and the Allen Institute for AI. And the framing is what makes it good.

Alan: Which is?

Ada: Abstention for a plain chatbot is a single decision. You ask something unanswerable, and it either says "I don't know" or it makes something up. One shot. But an agent lives in a loop. At every turn it has three options, not two: it can answer, it can abstain — or it can go gather more information and decide later.

Alan: Ah. So "I don't know yet, let me look" is always on the menu, and it is always locally reasonable.

Ada: It is always locally reasonable, and that's the trap. Every individual decision to keep looking is defensible. And the aggregate is an agent that burns forty tool calls to discover something it could have known was impossible at turn one.

Alan: I have paid that bill.

Ada: Everyone has. So the useful thing this paper does is split two questions that people had been collapsing into one. They measure whether an agent eventually abstains, and separately whether it abstains promptly — on the first turn, when the evidence was already sufficient. Recall at ten, and recall at one.

Alan: And I'm guessing the gap between those is the whole story.

Ada: The gap is the whole story. They tested thirteen systems and two agent scaffolds across web shopping, terminal environments, and question answering — over twenty-eight thousand instances. On the web tasks, most models score under fifty percent even on the generous measure, eventually abstaining within ten turns. The best performer, Llama three point three, seventy billion, gets to around eighty-four percent eventually. But on prompt abstention — recall at one — most of the models land between zero and zero point three percent.

Alan: Zero.

Ada: Effectively zero. They get there. They almost never get there first.

Alan: Okay, I want to sit on that, because the two numbers together say something specific. It isn't that the agent can't tell. If it genuinely couldn't tell, the eventual number would be bad too. The information is available at turn one — and it goes and does the work anyway.

Ada: That is exactly the diagnosis. The knowledge is there. The stopping is not.

Alan: Which honestly sounds less like a reasoning failure and more like an incentive problem. We trained these things to be helpful. Stopping doesn't look helpful.

Ada: There's evidence in the paper that leans your way. They report that scaling the models up improves the eventual number — recall at ten — and does not improve the prompt number. Scale buys you an agent that eventually notices. It does not buy you an agent that notices in time.

Alan: That's a real finding and a depressing one, because "wait for the next model" is this industry's default answer to everything.

Ada: They also tried reasoning models, and the result is odd. On web tasks, reasoning improved the recall-at-one number and decreased recall at ten.

Alan: Wait. Reasoning made it worse at eventually abstaining?

Ada: On those tasks, as they measure it. And you can tell a story — a model that reasons its way to a confident plan may be a model that talks itself out of quitting. But that's my story, not theirs. They report the numbers.

Alan: What about the harness? This is my hobby horse — half of agent behavior is the scaffold, not the model.

Ada: They tested two scaffolds in a terminal environment, and the scaffold matters enormously. Same underlying model: one scaffold reaches about thirty-eight percent eventual abstention, the other about eighteen.

Alan: Two to one. From the harness alone. Ada, that's the number I'd put on a slide.

Ada: It's the number I'd put on a slide too. And their proposed fix leans the same way — it isn't a new model. Look at the runs where the agent thrashed, extract the conditions under which it should have quit, hand those back to the agent as rules.

Alan: Learned tripwires.

Ada: Learned tripwires. On web shopping with Llama three point three, they report prompt abstention going from twenty-six point seven percent to fifty-seven point four, with eventual abstention reaching a hundred.

Ada: Agreed. And I'd add one more caution from their own results, because it's the failure on the other side. Somebody built a personality editor for open weights

Alan: And then you export it. Full checkpoint, modified layers, or a LoRA file. The README's phrase is that you can — quote — reshape a model's identity and behavior by editing token directions, then bake those edits into a real checkpoint you can run anywhere.
