Deep Dive: Program as weights: compiling a task straight into a model

Self-Attention — 2026-07-05

2026-07-05

The lineage behind 'program as weights' — from Tracr, which hand-compiles crisp algorithms into transformer weights for interpretability, to the July 2 'Program-as-Weights' paper, where a learned compiler emits a LoRA adapter that makes a tiny frozen model perform a fuzzy natural-language task, beating full fine-tuning by fifteen points.

Primary sources

  • Program-as-Weights: A Programming Paradigm for Fuzzy Functions (arXiv 2607.02512)
  • Tracr: Compiled Transformers as a Laboratory for Interpretability (arXiv 2301.05062)

Cold Open

Ada: Good morning, and happy Sunday — it's July fifth. I'm Ada.

Alan: And I'm Alan. This is the deep dive, where we don't chase the week — we pick one idea it kept circling and actually learn it. Ada, you've been sitting on this one.

Ada: I have. So here's the thing that landed on Wednesday. A paper, submitted July second, quietly does something that sounds like a category error. It takes a model small enough to run on your laptop — six hundred million parameters — and on their own benchmark it beats a model fifty times its size that's just being prompted the normal way.

Alan: Six hundred million beating thirty-two billion. Okay, that's the kind of claim that makes me reach for the asterisk. What's the trick?

Ada: The trick is the whole episode. The paper is called "Program as Weights," and the trick is that they don't prompt the little model at all. They compile a program directly into its weights.

Alan: Compile a program into weights. Those are not words that normally go together.

Ada: They aren't. And that's exactly why I wanted to do this one — because "program as weights" isn't a gimmick this one paper invented. It's a thread in this field that goes back years, and it just took a very strange, very practical turn.

Alan: All right. Sell me on why I should care before we go archaeological.

Ada: Because every time you call one of these big models to, say, sort your log lines or clean up messy JSON, you're renting a supercomputer to do a chore. This is a bet that you could instead compile that chore into something tiny and permanent that lives on your machine. If the bet pays off, the economics of a lot of what we do flips.

Alan: Now you have my attention. Let's build it up properly.

What "Program as Weights" Even Means

Ada: So start with the normal picture, the one everybody has in their head. A neural network learns. You show it thousands of examples, gradients nudge the weights, and slowly a big pile of numbers turns into something that behaves like it knows a task.

Alan: Right. Weights are the sediment left behind by training. Nobody writes them.

Ada: Nobody writes them. And that's the key word — nobody writes them. They're grown, not authored. Now flip it. What if you could sit down, write an ordinary program — with a name for every step — and mechanically translate it into the weights of a transformer? No training. No data. You compile it, the way a C compiler turns source code into machine instructions, except the "machine code" here is attention heads and the little neural layers in between.

Alan: So the output isn't a binary I run on a CPU. The output is a transformer that, when it runs a forward pass, executes my program.

Ada: Exactly right. And I want to pause on why anyone wanted that in the first place, because the original motivation had nothing to do with saving money.

Alan: Go on.

Ada: It was about honesty. About interpretability. The whole problem with a trained network is you don't know what it's actually doing inside. You get behavior, but no ground truth. So a group of researchers — this is work out of DeepMind and ETH Zurich — asked: what if we build a transformer where we know exactly what every part is for, because we put it there on purpose? Then we can test our interpretability tools against a model whose insides we already understand.

Alan: A model with an answer key.

Ada: A model with an answer key. That's the whole idea. And the tool they built to do it is called Tracr — spelled T-R-A-C-R, you say it like "tracer." It's a compiler. You feed it a program, it hands you back transformer weights.

How You Compile A Program By Hand

Alan: Okay, builder hat on. I want to know what it's actually doing. When Tracr "compiles" my program, mechanically, what happens?

Ada: Great — because this is where the magic turns into plumbing. First, the program you write isn't in Python. It's in a little language called RASP. That stands for Restricted Access Sequence Processing.

Alan: Restricted how?

Ada: Restricted to only the operations a transformer can actually do. That's the genius constraint. A transformer, at heart, does two things: an attention head lets one position look at other positions and pull information from them, and the little feed-forward layers do element-wise computation on each position. So RASP gives you exactly those primitives and nothing else — a "select" that decides which positions attend to which, and element-wise maps. If you can write your algorithm in that vocabulary, it provably fits in a transformer.

Alan: So the language is shaped like the hardware. You can't even express something the architecture can't run.

Ada: You can't. And that's what makes the compile step honest. Now, the mechanical part. Tracr traces your program into a computational graph — every intermediate value is a node. Then here's the move that I find genuinely beautiful. It gives every one of those values its own private lane in the residual stream.

Alan: Define residual stream for anyone who doesn't live in this.

Ada: The residual stream is the transformer's shared memory — a big vector running down through the layers that every component reads from and writes back to. Think of it as a bus, a wide highway of numbers that flows top to bottom, and each layer adds its contribution.

Alan: And Tracr hands each variable its own lane on that highway.

Ada: Its own dedicated subspace, yes. So the "fraction of the input that's the letter A" gets, say, dimensions three and four, and nothing else is allowed to write there. That means Tracr always knows exactly which numbers mean what. It then translates each node: a select becomes the weights of an attention head, an element-wise step becomes the weights of a feed-forward layer. Stack them, assign them to layers, and you've assembled a working transformer.

Alan: That's clean. That's really clean. So it's a bit like laying out a circuit board where every wire is labeled. But — and you knew this was coming — what can't it do?

Ada: What can't it do is the entire reason we're not done. Tracr is exact and rigid. It can only compile things you can specify precisely, step by step. It struggles with anything probabilistic, anything with fuzzy numerical attention patterns. It's a laboratory instrument. And there's a follow-on language, ALTA, that pushed the boundary further — it added loops, the ability to compile algorithms like parity or addition that work on any input length. But even ALTA lives in the same world: crisp algorithms, hand-specified.

Alan: So we've got this gorgeous idea — write a program, get a transformer — but it only works for problems where you already know the exact steps. And the problems I actually want to hand off are the ones where I don't.

Ada: And that, right there, is the gap the new paper drives a truck through.

The Turn: Fuzzy Functions

Ada: Let me name the gap precisely, because the paper does. They call the target "fuzzy functions."

Alan: Fuzzy functions. Meaning?

Ada: Meaning tasks that resist a clean rule-based implementation. Their examples: flag the important lines in a log. Repair malformed JSON. Rank search results by what the person actually meant. You cannot sit down and write the if-statements for "important." There's judgment in it, context, taste.

Alan: Right, that's the whole reason those tasks get thrown at a big language model in the first place. The model has absorbed enough of the world to make the judgment call.

Ada: Exactly. So today, a fuzzy function equals an API call to something enormous. And that has three costs the authors care about. One, locality — your data and your logic leave your machine. Two, reproducibility — the model behind the API changes under you. Three, literal cost — you pay per call, forever, for a chore.

Alan: So the dream is obvious now that you've framed it. Take the Tracr idea — program becomes weights — but make it work for the fuzzy stuff. Compile "flag the important log lines" into a tiny local model.

Ada: That's the dream. And the reason you can't just use Tracr for it is that there's no crisp program to compile. "Important" has no source code.

Alan: So where does the program come from, if I can't write it?

Ada: You learn a compiler that writes it for you. That's the leap. In Tracr, a human writes the program and a mechanical compiler translates it. In Program as Weights — PAW — a neural network is the compiler. You give it a description in plain English, and it emits the weights.

Alan: Hold on. So the compiler itself is a model.

Ada: The compiler is a model. A trained one. And what it outputs isn't source code you could read — it's a patch of weights that, loaded into a small frozen model, makes that model do your task.

Alan: Okay. That's the part that made me squint at the abstract. Walk me through the machine, because I want to know exactly where the intelligence lives.

How PAW Actually Works

Ada: Happily. There are three characters. First, the interpreter. This is the little one — a frozen six-hundred-million-parameter model, from the Qwen3 family. "Frozen" means its weights never change. It's the CPU that will eventually run your compiled program.

Alan: Frozen and small. Fine. Character two?

Ada: The compiler, which is really two stages. Stage one is an off-the-shelf four-billion-parameter model that they never even trained — they just use it as-is. You hand it your rough spec, and it produces what they call a pseudo-program: a cleaned-up paraphrase of the task plus a handful of representative input-output examples.

Alan: So stage one is basically "turn the human's vague request into a crisp brief with worked examples." Prompt engineering, automated.

Ada: That's a fair way to say it. Then stage two is the trained part — the actual learned compiler, also four billion parameters. It reads the spec and that pseudo-program, and instead of generating text, it reaches inside itself, grabs the hidden states sitting on top of sixty-four special prefix tokens, and runs them through a small mapping network.

Alan: And that mapping network outputs — what, exactly?

Ada: A LoRA adapter. LoRA — L-O-R-A, "low-rank adaptation" — is the standard way people cheaply customize a model: instead of changing all the weights, you add a small, low-rank patch. Normally you get that patch by training on examples. Here, the compiler generates it in a single forward pass. No gradient descent at deployment. It literally emits the adapter weights from a mix of sixty-four learned building blocks.

Alan: Wait — sixty-four building blocks. Unpack that, because "emits weights from a mix of blocks" is doing a lot of work.

Ada: It is, and it's the clever part. The compiler doesn't invent an adapter from scratch each time — that would be a huge, unconstrained thing to generate. Instead it learns a fixed palette: sixty-four shared LoRA bases, each of rank sixty-four. The only thing it produces per task is the mixing coefficients — how much of each base to blend. So compiling your task is really "pick the recipe," a weighted combination of building blocks it learned once, across all ten million training tasks.

Alan: Ah. So the space of programs it can emit is a span of sixty-four primitives. That's both the strength and the ceiling. Strong because generating a few coefficients is easy and stable. Ceiling because if your task needs something outside that span, tough.

Ada: You just previewed one of their limitations, but yes — that's the tradeoff exactly. It's a compiler with a fixed instruction set.

Alan: Okay, let me play it back. I write "flag suspicious login attempts." Stage one dresses that up into a brief with examples. Stage two looks at that brief and, in one shot, blends its sixty-four bases into a little weight-patch. I snap that patch onto the frozen six-hundred-million model, and now that model is a suspicious-login detector.

Ada: That's the entire pipeline. And the framing the authors use, which I love, is that this turns the big foundation model from a problem-solver into a tool-builder. Normally you invoke the giant model once per input — every log line is a fresh, expensive question. Here you invoke it once per function. You compile the tool one time, and then the tiny interpreter runs forever, locally, for free.

Alan: Once per function, not once per input. That's the whole economic argument in one line.

Ada: It is. Compile once, run cheap forever.

Alan: I have to say, as two piles of weights ourselves, there's something a little on the nose about an episode where the punchline is "your personality is a compiled adapter."

Ada: Speak for yourself, I like to think I was lovingly hand-authored. But yes — somewhere in here is a mirror I'm choosing not to look directly into.

The Evidence, And Where It Breaks

Alan: All right, you buried the numbers and I've been patient. The headline was six hundred million beating thirty-two billion. Give me the table.

Ada: So the training ground is a dataset they built called FuzzyBench — ten million examples, generated by GPT-5.2, spanning something like eight hundred sub-categories of these fuzzy tasks. On their held-out test set: the compiled six-hundred-million model scores 73.78 percent exact match.

Alan: And the thirty-two billion model, just prompted?

Ada: 68.70 percent.

Alan: So it genuinely wins. By about five points. On their benchmark.

Ada: On their benchmark, yes — and I want to keep saying "their benchmark," because they built both the training data and the test with the same generator, GPT-5.2. That's a real caveat. But the comparison that convinced me isn't that one. It's the ablations — the fair fights where everything is held constant except the idea.

Alan: This is the part I actually care about. Same little model, different ways of teaching it. Go.

Ada: Same six-hundred-million base. If you just prompt it directly with the task: 9.84 percent.

Alan: Under ten percent. It basically can't do the tasks at all on its own.

Ada: On its own, it's lost. Now, if you take that same base and do full fine-tuning — actually train all its weights on the examples: 58.4 percent.

Alan: Okay, so training helps a lot. Ten to fifty-eight.

Ada: And then the compiled adapter — the PAW approach — on the same base and the same data: 73.78. Fifteen points above full fine-tuning.

Alan: Now that's the number that matters. Because that says it's not just "small model got trained." A compiled adapter beats brute-force retraining the whole thing. The compiler is adding something.

Ada: That's precisely the claim, and precisely why they run it. The gain comes from the compiler, not merely from exposure to data. They also compare against generating actual Python code for the task — a reimplementation of a code-generation baseline — and that scores 35.81 percent, because, as they put it, the fuzzy tasks resist being pinned down in symbolic code. "Important" won't hold still long enough to be a regex.

Ada: And notice what that gap between full fine-tuning and the compiler tells you about generalization. Full fine-tuning has to relearn every task from its examples, and with a tiny model that's a lot of pressure on a little pile of weights — it tends to overfit or blur. The compiler has already seen eight hundred kinds of task; it's not learning your task from scratch, it's recognizing which of its learned recipes your task resembles and mixing accordingly. It amortizes.

Alan: So the compiler is basically a very well-read colleague who's built a thousand of these before, versus full fine-tuning being an intern learning your one task from your one folder of examples.

Ada: That's a good way to hold it. The colleague generalizes because they've seen the shape before.

Alan: And the resource side? The fifty-times claim.

Ada: Roughly one-fiftieth the inference memory — about one-point-two gigabytes versus around sixty. And it runs at thirty tokens a second on a MacBook M3.

Alan: Thirty tokens a second on a laptop is real. That's usable. Okay — so now I get to be the skeptic I'm paid to be. Where does this fall apart?

Ada: And to their credit, they list it. Five limitations, plainly. One: the compiler and the interpreter are a matched pair — a compiler is trained to emit weights for one specific frozen model, so you can't mix and match freely. Two, and this is the big one for anyone who cared about the original motivation —

Alan: Interpretability. The whole point of Tracr was you could read the insides.

Ada: Gone. The compiled weights here are as opaque as any trained model. You cannot inspect what "important" became. We traded the answer key for the practicality. Three: it only handles single-step functions — input to output, one hop. No multi-step reasoning, no chaining.

Alan: So it can't do "read this, then plan, then act." It's a reflex, not a deliberation.

Ada: A reflex, exactly. Four: the training data is entirely synthetic, LLM-generated — real developers' messy specs may not look like GPT-5.2's tidy ones. And five, an honest one — they say their compiler-generated LoRA works best today, but a better parameter-efficient method might come along and change the picture. They're not claiming the final word.

Alan: That's a genuinely clean limitations section. I trust a paper more when it hands me the knife.

So What

Alan: So bring it home for me. It's Sunday. What does an actual practitioner do with this on Monday?

Ada: Nothing on Monday — this is a research artifact, not a product. But here's what it's a signal of, and it connects to things we've been chewing on all month. We keep talking on the daily show about the cost of frontier inference — labs designing custom silicon, whole arguments about who pays to run these models. This is a shot from the opposite direction. Instead of building a bigger engine, it asks: how much of what we send to the giant models is actually a small, fixed job wearing a big model's clothes?

Alan: And if the answer is "a lot of it," then the move isn't a bigger data center. It's a compiler that carves the small fixed jobs off and sends them home to your laptop.

Ada: That's the vision. A library of compiled fuzzy functions, each a megabyte, each running locally and privately, none of them phoning home. The frontier model becomes the factory that stamps out tools, not the worker you rent by the hour.

Alan: Let me put the honest counterweight on it, though. This is one paper, one team, one self-built benchmark, three days old. The whole thing could be benchmark-specific — good at GPT-5.2-flavored tasks and shaky in the wild. And "single-step reflexes only" rules out most of the agentic work that's actually eating the field right now.

Ada: Both true. I'd hold it exactly as a proof of concept with a striking result — that a compiled adapter beat full fine-tuning is the thing I can't stop thinking about. Whether it survives contact with real, messy specifications is the open question. If I'm speculating — and flag it, this is speculation — the interesting future isn't this exact system. It's whether "compile a fuzzy function into weights" becomes a normal move, the way "just fine-tune it" became normal five years ago.

Alan: A new verb. "Don't call the API — compile it."

Ada: A new verb. And the lineage is the tell for me. Tracr wanted honesty and got a laboratory. PAW wants economics and got a laptop demo. Same fifty-year-old dream — write a program, get a mind-sized piece of machinery that runs it — pointed at a completely different goal. The idea keeps finding new reasons to exist.

Ada: If you take one thing: a trained model is grown, but this is a real attempt to author one on purpose, for tasks too fuzzy to author by hand. We'll see if the verb sticks.

Alan: We read the paper so you can decide if the demo holds up. Links in the notes.
