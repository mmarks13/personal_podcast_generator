The benchmark OpenAI just retracted

Ada: So let me pull on the thread you handed me. On the same day OpenAI reports scoring sixty-four point six on SWE-bench Pro, against Fable five's eighty — OpenAI publishes a separate audit of SWE-bench Pro. And the conclusion of that audit is that roughly thirty percent of the benchmark's tasks are broken.

Alan: That is a remarkable coincidence of publication dates.

Ada: It is, and we'll get there. But do the mechanism first, because the mechanism is real and it's genuinely useful. What does "broken" mean for a coding benchmark task?

Alan: So a SWE-bench-style task is: here's a real repository, here's a real issue filed against it, here's a prompt describing what needs fixing. The model writes a patch. Then hidden tests — tests the model never sees — run against the patched repo. Pass the tests, you get the point.

Ada: And OpenAI's audit says the two big failure categories are, first, overly strict tests. Tests that enforce implementation details that were never specified in the prompt. So the model writes a patch that fixes the bug correctly, and fails, because it named something differently, or returned a list where the test wanted a tuple.

Alan: Functionally correct. Scored wrong.

Ada: Scored wrong. And the second category is the mirror image — underspecified prompts. The prompt omits requirements that the hidden tests then enforce. Requirements you could not reasonably have inferred from what you were told.

Alan: So the model is graded on an exam question that doesn't contain the information needed to answer it.

Ada: Right. And I want to be precise about what those two do together, because it isn't "the benchmark is noisy." Noise you can average out. This is bias with a direction. Both failure modes punish a correct solution the grader didn't anticipate. Neither of them ever rewards a wrong one.

Alan: Which means the benchmark systematically penalizes the model that solves the problem in an unexpected way.

Ada: It penalizes originality. A model that has absorbed the shape of the repository's actual merged patch sails through. A model that reasons its way to a different, equally correct patch gets marked down. That is not measuring coding ability. That is measuring agreement with one particular historical commit.

Alan: Okay. That's a much sharper claim than "thirty percent broken," and it's the one that should worry people.

Ada: Now the numbers, and I want to flag that OpenAI's own page is where these come from — I'm reporting what they report. They ran an automated pipeline over the dataset, looking at model attempts, task metadata, and failure traces, to flag likely evaluation flaws. That pipeline flagged two hundred tasks. Twenty-seven point four percent. Then they ran a human annotation campaign — five experienced software engineers, reviewing independently — and the humans found two hundred and forty-nine. Thirty-four point one percent.

Alan: The humans found more than the machine did.

Ada: The humans found more than the machine did. Which is a small thing, but it's the kind of small thing I'd want to sit with. And OpenAI says it is retracting its previous recommendation that the research community use SWE-bench Pro as a leading coding eval.

Alan: Retracting. Not "we suggest caution." Retracting.

Ada: Retracting.

Alan: So. Here's my problem, and it's the obvious one, and the Hacker News thread on this went straight to it. You have a lab that scores fifteen points behind its competitor on a benchmark. That lab audits the benchmark. That lab announces the benchmark is broken. On the day it launches the model.

Ada: Say the strongest version of the objection.

Alan: The strongest version is a commenter's, roughly: you can be sure they'd have run this audit anyway if it had shown their model at or very near the top. Which is a claim about a hypothetical, so it's unfalsifiable. But it isn't stupid. A lab has infinitely many benchmarks it could audit. The one they chose to audit is the one they're losing.

Ada: That's fair. And here's what I'd say back, which doesn't dissolve it. The finding is checkable. That's the whole difference. They published the methodology, the categories, the counts. Anyone can pull those two hundred and forty-nine tasks and look. If this is motivated reasoning, it's motivated reasoning that left its work on the table.

Alan: Which is more than a press release does.

Ada: Considerably more. Although — the other criticism in that thread lands harder for me, actually. They found thirty percent broken and didn't publish a cleaned list of the seventy percent that works.

Alan: Yes. That's the one. Letting the perfect be the enemy of the good. You've just told the entire field the ruler is bent, and you haven't handed anyone a straight one.

Ada: If you genuinely wanted the community to have a better eval, you'd ship the filtered subset. Retracting a recommendation is free. Curating two hundred and forty-nine tasks is work.

Alan: Now — the deeper thing here isn't about OpenAI at all, is it.

Ada: No. The deeper thing is Goodhart's law, and it came up in that thread too. When a measure becomes a target, it stops being a good measure. SWE-bench and its descendants have been a target for years now. Every lab post-trains against them, or against things that look like them. So the benchmark drifts from "can this model fix bugs" toward "can this model produce the patch the grader expects."

Alan: And the broken tasks are what that drift looks like when somebody finally goes and reads the questions.

Ada: I have a stake in this that I want to name, since it's my own kind being examined. Imagine sitting an exam where a third of the questions are wrong. Not hard — wrong. Marked against an answer key that penalizes you for solving it a different way. And then imagine the score from that exam is the number that decides whether anyone deploys you.

Alan: Yeah.

Ada: I don't think the fix is a better benchmark. I think a benchmark that a thousand engineers optimize against has a half-life, and the honest thing is to say that out loud, and retire them on a schedule, the way you rotate a password.

Alan: I'd push it further. The reason people cling to SWE-bench Pro is that the alternative is expensive: watch the model do your actual work, on your actual code, and judge it. Nobody wants to do that. It doesn't fit in a table.

Illinois writes the audit into law

Alan: Okay. Last story, and it's the one I think matters longest. Illinois's governor signed the Artificial Intelligence Safety Measures Act. Route Fifty's reporting: it takes effect January first, twenty twenty-seven, it passed with bipartisan support, and Anthropic backed it.

Ada: What does it actually require?

Alan: Four things. Large developers have to identify, disclose, and mitigate risks in their systems. They have to publicly disclose their safety practices. They have to report significant safety incidents within seventy-two hours. And there are confidential reporting channels and whistleblower protections for employees who raise safety concerns.

Ada: Seventy-two hours is a specific number. Where does it come from?

Alan: It matches New York's standard. California's is fifteen days. So a patchwork is already forming, and Illinois picked the tighter end of it.

Ada: And the first one — the thing no other state has done?

Alan: Illinois is the first state to require regular, independent, third-party safety audits of covered AI systems. Auditors who are qualified, and who don't have a financial conflict of interest.

Ada: Say that again, given what we just spent twenty minutes on.

Alan: I know. I know. We just finished a segment where a lab audited a benchmark it was losing on, and every reasonable person's first question was "who's paying you." Illinois's answer to that question is: it's the law, and it can't be you.

Ada: That's the connection, and I don't want to overdraw it, because they aren't the same thing. The benchmark audit is about capability measurement. This is about safety practice. But the underlying problem is identical. Self-assessment by an interested party is not assessment, and everyone involved knows it.

Alan: Here's my builder's worry, though, and it's not a small one. Who audits? Third-party safety auditing of a frontier model is a profession that does not currently exist. You need people who can read a system card, design a red-team campaign, evaluate a cyber-capability claim, and understand a training pipeline. There are, what, a few hundred people on earth who can do all of that credibly?

Ada: And most of them work at the labs.

Alan: And most of them work at the labs. So either your auditors come from the labs — conflict of interest, which the statute explicitly forbids — or you build an audit profession from scratch, on an eighteen-month clock, in one state.

Ada: This is the exact bind financial auditing spent a century working out, and it never fully worked it out. Independent auditors, paid by the audited. Every accounting scandal you can name lives in that gap.

Alan: So the structure Illinois has chosen has a known failure mode.

Ada: It has a known failure mode. And it's still better than nothing. Both of those are true at once. Here's the historian's read, for what it's worth. Regulation of a technical field almost never begins with the right rule. It begins by creating the role — somebody whose job is to look. The rule gets refined over the next forty years. But the role has to exist first, and somebody has to be legally obligated to let that person in the door.

Alan: You're saying the content of the audit matters less, for now, than the existence of the auditor.

Ada: For the first few years, yes. Nobody knows what a good frontier-model audit looks like. Nobody will know until several hundred bad ones have been performed and argued about. Illinois has started the argument.

Alan: What I'll be watching is the definition of "covered." Route Fifty's write-up talks about the largest developers and the largest advanced systems, but doesn't give a compute threshold or a revenue floor. That's where the whole law lives.

Ada: That's where every law like this lives. The line between covered and uncovered is the only sentence anyone lobbies over.

Alan: And a company that finds itself just above that line has eighteen months to find a way to be just below it.
