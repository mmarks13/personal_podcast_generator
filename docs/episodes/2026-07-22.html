Knowing when to stop

Alan: One more, and it's a paper. It's called "Agentic Abstention: Do Agents Know When to Stop Instead of Act?" — Han Luo, Bingbing Wen and Lucy Lu Wang, out of the University of Washington, Leeds, Southwest Jiaotong and the Allen Institute. It's been near the top of the weekly paper list for a while now, so it's had time to be looked at by people who aren't me.

Ada: Abstention meaning refusing to answer?

Alan: That's the version you know, and the paper's argument is that that version is the wrong shape for agents. Single-turn abstention is: here's a question, answer it or say you don't know. One decision. Agentic abstention is a sequential decision. At every turn the agent can answer, abstain, or go gather more information — and their key sentence is that the need to abstain, quote, "may only become clear after interacting with the environment."

Ada: Ah. So the task looks fine until it isn't.

Alan: The task looks completely reasonable. "Buy me a red waterproof jacket under sixty dollars." Nothing wrong with that sentence. It's just that in this particular store, that jacket does not exist, and the only way to find that out is to go shopping.

Ada: A person figures that out in about four clicks and says "they don't have it."

Alan: A person says "they don't have it." The agent keeps shopping. So they built a benchmark to measure exactly that. Three settings. WebShop: a thousand tasks, five hundred solvable, five hundred where the correct answer is to stop. Terminal-Bench 2.0: two hundred seventy-seven tasks, eighty-nine solvable, a hundred and eighty-eight where you should stop. And an adapted question-answering set of about twenty-seven thousand. Thirteen model-as-agent systems, two real agent scaffolds — Terminus 2 and Codex CLI. More than twenty-eight thousand tasks all in.

Ada: And the headline number?

Alan: Here's where it gets good, because they split one metric into two, and the split is the whole contribution. Overall recall: did the agent eventually abstain. Timely recall: did it abstain without a pile of pointless tool calls first. On the web setting, the best system — Llama 3.3, the seventy-B — gets eighty-three point two percent overall.

Ada: Which sounds fine.

Alan: Which sounds fine. Timely recall for that same system is twenty-six point seven percent.

Ada: Oh, that's an ugly gap.

Alan: That's the paper. Eight times in ten it gets there. Two and a half times in ten it gets there before wasting your money. On the terminal setting, the best configuration is twenty-one point six percent timely.

Ada: So the failure was never "the agent doesn't know." It's "the agent knows late."

Alan: Knows late, and you pay for the interval. And here's the finding I didn't expect. The authors report that model scale doesn't reliably help — larger or more capable models are sometimes worse at abstaining in time. And reasoning cuts both ways: it improves timeliness and reduces overall recall.

Ada: That one I can explain, and I want to, because it isn't a paradox. If you train a system to try hard, then "try hard" and "keep trying" are the same gradient. Every capability improvement we celebrate is, mechanically, an improvement at not giving up. Stopping is the one competence that sits directly against what we optimize for.

Alan: And neither of us is in a position to be smug about it.

Ada: We are not. Which is where I want to poke the paper, because I have two problems with it. The first is the roster. Look at the thirteen systems: Llama, Qwen, GLM, Gemma, GPT-OSS, one small hosted model. That's mostly the open mid-tier. I can't tell from this whether the frontier coding agents people actually deploy fail the same way. Their own finding — that scale doesn't help — is an argument that they might. It isn't a measurement of them.

Alan: Accepted. Though I'd say the scaffold matters as much as the model here, and they did test two real ones.

Ada: Second problem is their fix. They propose a method they call CONVOLVE — context engineering, no retraining. They take full interaction trajectories and distill them into reusable stopping rules, and put those in the context. On WebShop, Llama seventy-B's timely recall goes from twenty-six point seven to fifty-seven point four.

Alan: Which is a very large lift for a prompt-level intervention.

Ada: And overall recall goes from eighty-three point two to a hundred percent. A hundred. When a benchmark goes to a hundred, my first thought is not "solved." It's that the unsolvable cases in this particular benchmark have a tell, and we may have just taught the model the tell.

Alan: That's a fair worry, and the honest answer is: on this benchmark, in this store. Stopping rules distilled from WebShop trajectories are, to some degree, rules about WebShop.

Ada: So what does a listener actually do with it tonight?

Alan: Two things, both cheap. One: instrument your failed agent runs separately from your successful ones. The cost of running agents isn't the average run, it's the tail of things it should have quit and didn't, and most dashboards average that away. Two: the stopping-rules idea is an afternoon's work. You don't need their method. Go read twenty of your agent's worst runs, write down the conditions under which it obviously should have stopped, and put those in the prompt.

Ada: And say the quiet part about the metric.

Alan: The quiet part is that nearly every agent benchmark is built out of tasks that are possible. This one deliberately fills half the set with tasks that aren't, and the scores fall through the floor. That gap isn't a flaw in the models. It's a gap in what we've been measuring all along.
