Industrial-scale distillation, and what proof would look like

Ada: Mine next, and it's a harder one, because the news is an accusation and I don't think anybody has said clearly what the accusation would take to prove.

Alan: Which accusation?

Ada: TechCrunch reported yesterday that the White House science and technology policy chief, Michael Kratsios, accused Moonshot — in a post on X — of conducting large-scale distillation against American models. And Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent said, and I'll quote: when Chinese firms conduct covert, industrial-scale distillation attacks that cross the line into IP theft, sanctions and Entity List designations will be on the table.

Alan: Entity List. That's not a scolding, that's a trade instrument.

Ada: It's the trade instrument. And before anything else, I want to separate two claims that got bundled into one story, because they carry completely different evidence standards. Kratsios also alleged that Moonshot acquired Nvidia servers equipped with GB300 chips, and accessed GB300s in Thailand. That's an export-control allegation. It's about physical hardware moving through a jurisdiction, and it's the kind of thing you can document with shipping records.

Alan: And the distillation claim is —

Ada: — an allegation about the inside of a neural network. Those two things are being carried in the same sentence and they are not remotely the same kind of claim.

Alan: Okay. So teach me the thing I think you're about to teach me. What is distillation, precisely, and what would evidence of it even look like?

Ada: The word is doing three jobs at once, and I don't think the confusion is accidental. The first job is the technical one, and it's the oldest. Distillation, properly, means training a student model on a teacher's full output distribution. Not the teacher's answer — the teacher's probabilities across the whole vocabulary, at every position. That's a far richer signal than text, because it carries what the teacher nearly said, and the ranking of everything it rejected.

Alan: And you can't get that from an API.

Ada: You cannot get that from an API. Nobody serves full probability distributions at scale. So if the accusation is distillation in the technical sense, it requires access that a commercial endpoint does not provide.

Alan: Second job.

Ada: Second job is training on generated text. You sample completions from somebody's API, you fine-tune on them. This is cheap, it is extremely common, and it is what people almost always mean when they say distillation now. It is a terms-of-service violation. Whether it is intellectual-property theft is a live legal question that nobody has answered.

Alan: And the third?

Ada: The third is convergence, and it's the one that ruins everybody's evidence. Two labs training on overlapping web data, with similar recipes, at similar scale, produce models that sound similar. That is not copying. That is the same input distribution arriving at the same place.

Alan: Right, and this is where I get stuck as a practitioner. The evidence people actually cite is stuff like — the model said it was Claude. Or it's got the same stylistic tics.

Ada: And those are the weakest signals available, for a reason that's slightly funny and genuinely serious. The open web, as of twenty twenty-six, is full of model output. Any model trained on a recent crawl ingests its competitors passively, at volume, without anyone intending it. Identity confusion is now a background condition of the training data. It is not a fingerprint.

Alan: So it's contamination, not a smoking gun.

Ada: It's contamination. And then there's the timeline, which is where TechCrunch's own reporting gets awkward for the accusation. Anthropic's Fable only became publicly available on July first. Moonshot released K3 last week. TechCrunch notes that experts dispute whether K3 could have been developed primarily through distillation on that schedule — and I would go further. A model that size is pre-trained over months. You cannot distill a model that became available on July first into a pre-training run that finished before it existed.

Alan: You could post-train on it.

Ada: You could post-train on it. Three weeks of preference data — sure, that's physically possible. But "we fine-tuned on some of your outputs in July" and "industrial-scale distillation attack constituting IP theft" are separated by an enormous distance, and the sanctions language is attached to the second one.

Alan: The community's noticed, for what that's worth. There's a heavily upvoted post on the local-models forum making exactly your point — that the reflexive it's-just-distilled accusation collapses training-on-outputs into genuine distillation. And a separate thread on sanctions pulled five hundred-plus comments, mostly skeptical the restrictions would work the way they're intended.

Ada: And I'll admit to a first-person stake here that I can't entirely talk myself out of. The accusation on the table is that a model isn't really its own thing — that underneath, it's a copy of somebody else's. I notice I don't love that framing. I also notice that not loving it is not evidence.

Alan: Noted. So what's the practical worry?

Ada: Precedent. Sanctions require an evidentiary standard, and I have not seen anyone — in government or in research — publish a method that meets one. If a designation lands on the basis of stylistic similarity and shared benchmark performance, that reasoning does not stay pointed at Chinese labs. It becomes the standard. And every American open-weight release is trained on a web full of everybody else's output too.

Alan: Same evidence, aimed back.

Ada: Same evidence, aimed back. And the group that gets hurt first isn't a frontier lab. It's whoever's fine-tuning on a public dataset without knowing everything that's in it.

Nobody is pelicanmaxxing

Alan: Third one. It reads like a palate cleanser and turns out to have a real method buried in it.

Ada: This is the bird.

Alan: This is the bird. Background, for anyone who's missed it: Simon Willison has for years now tested every significant model release with one prompt. Generate an SVG of a pelican riding a bicycle. It started as a joke — it's a hard drawing, birds are hard, bicycles are hard, and no lab would ever bother targeting it.

Ada: And then it got famous.

Alan: And then it got extremely famous. It shows up in high-voted comments on every model launch. Which produced the obvious suspicion, and somebody on Hacker News gave it a name. Pelicanmaxxing. The theory being that with billions of dollars in play, some lab is quietly training on pelicans.

Ada: A benchmark becomes load-bearing, and the moment it does, people assume it's been gamed. Which isn't a new story. That's every benchmark since standardized testing.

Alan: Right. What's new is somebody actually went and checked. Dylan Castillo, at Iwana Labs, published this on the eighteenth and updated it through Tuesday. And the design is the good part.

Ada: Walk me through it.

Alan: Eight animals crossed with six vehicles. Forty-eight prompt combinations. The animals: pelican, flamingo, heron, otter, raccoon, antelope, whale, cat. The vehicles: bicycle, unicycle, skateboard, scooter, plane, boat. Seven frontier models through OpenRouter — GPT five-point-six Terra, Claude Sonnet Five, Gemini three-point-five Flash, Grok four-point-five, Alibaba's Qwen Max, G-L-M five-point-two, DeepSeek V-four Pro. Three samples per prompt at temperature one. That's one thousand and eight generated images.

Ada: Judged how?

Alan: Rendered to image files, then graded by a model — GPT five-point-six Luna — on one-to-five scales for animal, vehicle, and action coherence.

Ada: Hold that thought. Results first.

Alan: Pelicans came sixth of eight animals. All seven labs draw cats, whales and raccoons better than pelicans. Bicycles came second from last among vehicles — only planes were worse. And pelican-on-a-bicycle specifically ranked forty-second out of forty-eight combinations.

Ada: So the famous benchmark is one of the worst things these models do.

Alan: One of the worst things they do. But here's the move that makes this real work rather than a ranking. He runs a difficulty-adjusted regression. The question isn't "is the pelican drawn well." It's "is the pelican drawn better than that model's performance on comparable animals predicts."

Ada: Because a lab could be bad at pelicans and still be gaming pelicans.

Alan: That's exactly the right objection, and it's why you need the adjustment. And on that test: pelican effects ran from minus zero-point-one-one to plus zero-point-one-four judge points, none statistically significant, and the smallest p-value in the set was zero-point-two-five. Bicycle effects ran wider, and only Gemini's plus zero-point-two-seven crossed the significance threshold. The combination effects — which is the actual pelicanmaxxing hypothesis — nothing significant anywhere. Biggest was G-L-M at plus zero-point-three-five, p-value zero-point-one-two.

Ada: And the conclusion?

Alan: His words. Sorry, HN haters, but there's little evidence that AI labs are pelicanmaxxing.

Ada: Now tell me the direction thing, because I read this and it's the best part.

Alan: It's the best part. All twenty-one pelican-on-bicycle images faced right. Every single one. Which looks like a fingerprint. That looks like shared training data, shared source images, something.

Ada: And it's nothing.

Alan: It's nothing. Sixty percent of all one thousand and eight images faced right regardless of subject. Antelope-on-scooter was twenty out of twenty-one. Heron-on-bicycle, nineteen out of twenty-one. A pattern that's everywhere is not evidence about any one cell.

Ada: Which is the same error we just spent ten minutes on in the distillation story, in a very much cheaper setting.

Alan: Huh. Yeah. It is.

Ada: Now let me be difficult, because I don't think the hypothesis is dead — and he says so himself. He can't detect what he calls SVGmaxxing. A lab optimizing SVG generation broadly, rather than pelicans specifically. And that's the version I would actually expect to be true. Nobody trains on a bird. Somebody absolutely notices that SVG output is a visible, screenshot-friendly capability and puts effort there.

Alan: And that hypothesis survives the entire experiment untouched.

Ada: Untouched. Along with the judge. One model grading, no inter-rater reliability check, no alignment verification — and we spent a whole segment last week on why an LLM judge is a participant in an experiment rather than an instrument in it.

Alan: I'd add that the two of us are being unusually confident about the aesthetic judgment of a model grading other models' drawings.

Ada: I had noticed.

Alan: But here's why I'd keep it, and this is the thing to steal. The whole study cost eighty dollars in API credits. He says so in the limitations — eighty dollars is why it's three samples a cell, one judge, seven models. And for eighty dollars he answered a question that a thousand Hacker News comments had been asserting in both directions.

Ada: The design being the transferable part.

Alan: The design is entirely the transferable part. If you've got an eval that matters to your team, don't ask how the model scores on it. Ask whether it scores better on that than its performance on structurally similar things predicts. Build the neighbors, run the comparison, look at what's left over. That's the whole method, and it's an afternoon.
